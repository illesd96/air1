In August 1915 the British Admiralty asked Vickers Limited to take the troubled 9r rigid airship design and stretch it into something actually usable. The result, drawn by chief designer H.B. Pratt with young Barnes Wallis as his assistant, was the 23 class: 535 feet long, lifted by hydrogen across 17 gas cells, and powered by four Rolls-Royce Eagle engines slung in gondolas. Four airships were built between 1917 and 1918 by Vickers at Barrow-in-Furness, William Beardmore on the Clyde and Armstrong Whitworth on the Tyne. They were too late to make a real wartime difference but they patrolled the North Sea on U-boat watch, taught the Royal Naval Air Service how to operate rigid airships at sea, and most importantly they trained the engineering team that would later draw the R100 under Wallis's leadership. We walk through the 23-class's structural lessons, why hydrogen was both the only fuel available and the obvious safety problem, how the ships compared to the contemporary Zeppelins, and how Barnes Wallis's career arc out of Vickers's airship shed eventually produced the bouncing bomb.
