Aerion Corporation unveiled the SBJ in 2004 with a deceptively simple pitch: cross the Atlantic in three and a half hours in a 12-seat private jet cruising at Mach 1.6. Behind that pitch sat real engineering. Aerion had spent years working with NASA on a natural laminar flow wing thin enough and clean enough to make sustained supersonic cruise economical. Billionaire Robert Bass underwrote two decades of work, Pratt and Whitney offered the JT8D-219, and partnership announcements with Lockheed Martin and Boeing pumped credibility. The SBJ matured into the larger AS2 in 2014. Then nothing flew. In May 2021, Aerion abruptly closed shop with over a billion dollars spent and not a single airframe completed. We trace what the natural laminar flow wing actually delivered in the wind tunnel, how the AS2 was supposed to bridge ICAO sonic boom rules, why aerospace primes signed on and then drifted away, and what Aerion's collapse means for Boom and the rest of the next supersonic generation.
