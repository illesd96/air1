Chris Heintz drew the CH 2000 trainer for Zenair in Quebec, then handed the design to a new company called Aircraft Manufacturing and Design (AMD), which set up first in Saint-Hubert and later relocated to Eastman, Georgia. The aircraft became known commercially as the Alarus: a two-seat, side-by-side, low-wing all-metal trainer with a 116-horsepower Lycoming O-235 and a control feel pitched somewhere between a Cessna 152 and a Cherokee 140. AMD's quietest big success came when the Mexican Air Force chose the Alarus as its primary trainer and ordered 25 in the early 2000s, designated as the AMD CH 2000-T and used at the Mexican Air Force Academy in Zapopan. Civilian sales never approached Cessna or Piper volumes, but AMD shipped trainers across the Americas and built a respectable owner community. We look at the Heintz design philosophy, what differs between the Zenair and AMD-era airframes, how the Mexican fleet has aged, and why the Alarus remains a competitive choice for ab initio schools today.
