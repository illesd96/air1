Flug- und Fahrzeugwerke Altenrhein on the Swiss shore of Lake Constance partnered with Italy's Savoia-Marchetti in the late 1960s on a clean-sheet primary trainer for the European market. The result was the AS-202 Bravo: a two- to three-seat, low-wing, all-metal trainer powered by a 180-horsepower Lycoming AEIO-360, fully aerobatic, with a sliding bubble canopy and a delightfully precise control feel. It first flew on 7 March 1969 and entered production through the 1970s and 1980s. Civilian sales were modest but the Bravo proved unexpectedly popular as a military trainer: the Iraqi Air Force, Indonesian Air Force, Moroccan Air Force, Ugandan Air Force and several others ordered it. Switzerland itself, awkwardly, never adopted it as a service trainer. About 200 were built between FFA and SIAI-Marchetti before production ended. We walk through the AS-202's three main variants, the Bravo's odd export buyer list, what its handling really feels like compared to a Bulldog or a SF.260, and where surviving airframes can still be found today.
