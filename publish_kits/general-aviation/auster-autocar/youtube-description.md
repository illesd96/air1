When peace broke out in 1945, the Rearsby factory in Leicestershire pivoted from building Auster air observation post aircraft for the RAF to building a postwar four-seater for civilian Britain. The J/5 Autocar emerged in 1949: a high-wing, four-seat taildragger sharing structural DNA with the prewar British Taylorcraft licence and, by extension, with the American Piper Cub. Roughly 28 were built before the model gave way to later Auster types, and the Autocar drifted into the corner of the type market reserved for collectors. Yet its siblings flew air observation post duties in Korea, Malaya and Suez, making the family the unsung British counterpart to the American L-4. Auster Aircraft merged into Beagle in 1960, ending a thirty-year run of distinctly British taildraggers. We trace the bloodline from Taylorcraft to Rearsby to Beagle, look at what surviving Autocars cost today, why British vintage fly-ins still treat them as a quiet star turn, and how owners keep parts moving in 2026.
