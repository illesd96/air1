Before the Bell 47 hung in the lobby of MoMA, there was the Bell 30. Three prototypes, hand-built in 1942-43, designed by Arthur M. Young — a Princeton-educated philosopher-turned-engineer who'd spent a decade flying model helicopters in a barn.

In this video: how Young's teetering rotor and stabilizer bar solved the control problems that had stumped everyone before him, why Larry Bell bet a fighter-plane factory on a helicopter, and how Ship 1A — the bubble-canopy version — became the immediate parent of the production Bell 47.
