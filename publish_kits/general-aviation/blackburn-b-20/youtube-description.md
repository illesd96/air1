Flying boat designers have always paid a penalty for the deep planing hull they need on the water and the dead weight they have to carry once airborne. Blackburn Aircraft's chief designer John Douglas Rennie tried to solve that. His patent described a lower hull section that hinged upward in flight, leaving the aircraft with a clean fuselage and a conventional wing-mounted float arrangement once airborne. The Blackburn B-20 prototype embodied the idea: a four-engine flying boat first flown in March 1940 from Dumbarton on the Clyde. The retractable hull worked on its early water trials. On 7 April 1940, however, the prototype crashed during high-speed handling tests, killing most of the crew. The Air Ministry, already committed to the Sunderland, never ordered a second airframe and the concept died with the wreckage. We look at how Rennie's hull actually moved, what wind tunnel data the test programme returned before the accident, and why retractable-hull flying boats never made another serious appearance.
