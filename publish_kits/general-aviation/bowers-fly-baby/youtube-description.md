Peter M. Bowers was an unlikely homebuilder. By day he was an engineer at Boeing in Seattle, by avocation the foremost historian of Boeing's aircraft, and on weekends a designer with a passion for the simplest possible plans-built aeroplane. In 1962 his Fly Baby won the EAA Design Contest at Rockford with a brutally honest brief: a single-seat, wood and fabric, open-cockpit, low-wing monoplane that an absolute beginner could build with hand tools and a few hundred dollars of plywood. The wings folded for trailering. Power could be anything from a 65-horsepower Continental A-65 up through a 100-horsepower O-200. Bowers sold the plans for a handful of dollars and the Fly Baby quietly became one of the most-built American homebuilt designs of the 1960s and 1970s. We trace Bowers's design philosophy, walk through what makes a Fly Baby a Fly Baby, look at the biplane Fly Baby 1A variant, and meet builders who are still finishing first airframes from the original 1962 plans today. Builders welcome in the comments.
