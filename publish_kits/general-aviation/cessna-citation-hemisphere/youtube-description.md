In November 2015 Cessna stood up the biggest Citation it had ever proposed. The Hemisphere would have stretched the line into the super-midsize and large-cabin segments with a Mach 0.90 cruise, a 4,500 nautical mile range, and a 102-inch flat-floor cabin meant to embarrass the competition. The catch was a single supplier dependency: Safran's brand new Silvercrest turbofan, also chosen by Dassault for the Falcon 5X. When the engine slipped repeatedly on weight and thermal margins, Dassault walked first. Cessna paused Hemisphere development in April 2018 and never restarted it. We walk through the spec the Hemisphere was chasing, why the Silvercrest's problems were terminal for both airframes, what Textron salvaged in its product strategy, and what this teaches about engine risk on clean-sheet bizjets. Aerospace risk management in one anecdote.
