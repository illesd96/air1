When the FAA carved out Part 103 in the early 1980s and the American ultralight market exploded, John Chotia had already been at it for five years. His Weedhopper, introduced in 1977, was conceived as the simplest possible powered aircraft: an aluminum tube fuselage frame, a single high wing braced with king-post cables, tricycle gear, an open seat, and a small two-stroke engine driving a wooden prop. Controls were two-axis only, with stick-induced wing warping for roll and rudder for yaw, no aileron mechanism at all. The kit assembled in 25 to 30 hours and sold for a fraction of any certificated trainer. Thousands left the factory. The design changed hands several times through the 1980s, eventually surfacing as the Weedhopper of Utah. We walk through the Weedhopper's structural simplicity, the two-axis control story, how the type sits inside Part 103 today, what restored airframes are worth and what to inspect on a 1980s example. Ultralight pilots welcome in the comments.
