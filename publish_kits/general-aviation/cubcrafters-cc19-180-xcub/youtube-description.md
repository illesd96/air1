CubCrafters had spent decades rebuilding Piper Super Cubs in Yakima, Washington when it finally drew its own from a clean sheet. The CC19-180 XCub was introduced at Sun n Fun in 2016 with a 180-horsepower Lycoming O-360, an all-new chromoly steel fuselage, fuel tanks in the wings, a glass panel and a useful load that finally matched what backcountry pilots actually carry. Most importantly the XCub went the Part 23 certified route rather than experimental, so insurance, financing and training fit neatly into the modern GA system. Variants followed: the FX-3 quick-build experimental, the Carbon Cub family, and the float-rated SS. We unpack the Cub family tree, walk through what makes the XCub different from a refurbished Super Cub, look at the typical Idaho backcountry mission profile, and explain why CubCrafters now ships hundreds of airframes a year.
