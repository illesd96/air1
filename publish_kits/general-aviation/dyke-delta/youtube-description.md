John Dyke flew the JD-1 Delta prototype in 1962 and refined it into the JD-2 a few years later, a four-seat tailless delta meant for amateur construction. The structure is pure Dyke: SAE 4130 steel tube spaceframe under a fabric-and-fiberglass skin, with retractable tricycle gear and a clever wing-folding scheme that lets the panels hinge upward and lie flat across the top of the fuselage for trailering. Performance is unusually good for the era, mid-170s on around 180 horsepower out of a Lycoming, with low-speed handling tamed by full-span elevons. Fewer than fifty were ever finished, but the type remains a fixture at Oshkosh and a magnet for the homebuilt avant garde. We look at Dyke's design philosophy, the structural tricks that make the delta work, why so few were completed, and what surviving builders say about flying one cross-country today.
