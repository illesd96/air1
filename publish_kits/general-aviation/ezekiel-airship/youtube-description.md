In 1900 a Baptist preacher and sawmill operator named Burrell Cannon convinced the citizens of Pittsburg, Camp County, Texas to capitalise the Ezekiel Air Ship Manufacturing Company. The design, which Cannon insisted he had derived directly from the wheel-within-a-wheel imagery in the Book of Ezekiel, used four large paddle wheels driven by a four-cylinder gasoline engine. The full-scale airship was completed by 1902. According to unverified eyewitness accounts collected decades later, it lifted briefly from a Pittsburg pasture in late 1902, more than a year before the Wright Brothers flew at Kitty Hawk. No documentation survives to confirm this, and the airship itself was destroyed in 1903 when it was being shipped by rail to the St Louis World's Fair and was blown off its flatcar in a storm. We tell the Ezekiel story carefully, separate what can be sourced from what is folklore, look at how the Texas Historical Commission has handled the marker outside Pittsburg, and consider what early aviation experimentation in rural America really looked like.
