Facebook, now Meta, designed and flew the Aquila as part of its Connectivity Lab effort to provide internet access to remote regions. The aircraft was a carbon composite flying wing with a 42 metre wingspan, solar cells over the entire upper surface, four electric motors, and a target endurance measured in months at altitudes around 18,000 to 20,000 metres. Aquila first flew on 28 June 2016 at Yuma Proving Ground in Arizona, where the maiden flight ended with a damaged landing gear during touchdown that the NTSB attributed to autopilot behavior in a gust. A second prototype flew successfully in 2017, but in June 2018 Facebook ended internal development, choosing instead to partner with industrial HAPS specialists. We look at why a 42-metre solar wing is harder than it looks, how Aquila compares with Google's Loon and Airbus Zephyr, and what Meta learned about staying out of aerospace primes' territory. A cautionary tale in tech-meets-aerospace.
