The Gulfstream American GA-7 Cougar is an all-metal four-seat twin-engined light aircraft produced in Savannah, Georgia between 1977 and 1979. The airframe is a direct evolution of the Gulfstream American AA-5B Tiger, itself a development of the AA-1 Yankee Clipper, which in turn was Jim Bede's BD-1. That bonded-honeycomb construction lineage gives the Cougar a stiff, light airframe, and its two Lycoming O-320 engines give roughly 150 knot cruise on combined fuel burns that compare favorably with a single. Just 115 Cougars were built before Gulfstream American shut the line, leaving the Cougar a rare bird with an unusually tight type club. We look at what carried over from the AA-5 and what didn't, why the Cougar has a soft spot in twin-rating training programs, the parts support situation today, and what to inspect for spar and bonded-joint condition on a 45-year-old airframe.
