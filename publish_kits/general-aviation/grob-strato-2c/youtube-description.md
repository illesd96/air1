In 1989 the German Aerospace Center asked for a piston-engine atmospheric research aircraft that could carry a payload at 24 kilometres for hours at a time. Grob Aircraft in Tussenhausen-Mattsies answered with the Strato 2C: a twin turbocharged 400-horsepower piston design with a wing of nearly 57 metres span, all composite, and a glider-like aspect ratio that maximised lift in the thin stratospheric air. The single prototype first flew in 1995. After a difficult flight test programme that exposed engine cooling and turbocharger problems, the Strato 2C set the world altitude record for piston-engine aircraft at 60,897 feet on 4 August 1995 over Manching. That flight was also its last: the DLR cancelled the programme citing cost overruns and the technical compromises of the piston configuration. The single airframe was donated to the Deutsches Museum and now hangs in their Oberschleissheim collection. We walk through the engineering choices, what the Strato 2C actually proved about high-altitude piston propulsion, and what its data still contributes to modern stratospheric platform design.
