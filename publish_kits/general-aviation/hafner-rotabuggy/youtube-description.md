By 1942 the British Airborne Forces wanted to drop wheeled vehicles into combat alongside their gliderborne infantry. Austrian-born engineer Raoul Hafner, working at the Airborne Forces Experimental Establishment at Sherburn-in-Elmet, came up with what he called the Rotabuggy: a standard Willys MB Jeep with a free-spinning rotor mast bolted above the bonnet, a tail boom for stability, and a small bubble canopy to keep the driver-pilot out of the slipstream. The Jeep would be towed behind a Whitley bomber as an autogyro, then released to glide to the ground, where the rotor would be discarded and the Jeep driven away. The Rotabuggy first flew on 16 November 1943 behind a Bedford QL truck on a runway, then took to the air behind a Whitley. It reached 70 miles per hour in trials. Just as the programme was maturing, large transport aircraft like the C-47 Skytrain offered enough hold space to carry a Jeep internally and the Rotabuggy was quietly abandoned. We walk through the design, the trials data, and the surviving footage that proves it actually flew.
