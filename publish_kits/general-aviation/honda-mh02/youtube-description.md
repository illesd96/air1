Honda has never been a one-product company, but the road to the HondaJet started further back than most pilots realise. In the late 1980s Honda Research and Development sent a small team to Mississippi State University's Raspet Flight Research Laboratory with a mandate to learn how to build a jet. The result was the MH02, an all-composite, twin-turbofan, T-tail business jet with the engines mounted high on aft pylons. The prototype was completed in 1992 and first flew on 5 March 1993. It was never offered for sale. Instead it taught Honda how composite fuselage layups behave under jet loads, how a small honeyed cabin actually pressurises, and how unconventional engine mounting affects handling. Twenty years later the HondaJet HA-420 emerged with the now-famous over-the-wing engine mount and stretched the lessons from the MH02 into production. We walk through what Honda actually learned at Raspet, why the MH02 itself never reached market, and how its DNA flowed into Greensboro. Honda fans and bizjet nerds welcome in the comments.
