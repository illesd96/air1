In the late 1950s the British Ministry of Aviation wanted to know whether jet bleed air piped along a wing's trailing edge could let a fighter-sized jet land at helicopter approach speeds. Hunting Aircraft answered with the H.126, a single-engine, single-seat research jet first flown in 1963. The wing had no conventional flaps. Instead, a slotted trailing edge dumped engine bleed air at right angles to the chord, energising the boundary layer and curving the lift vector downward. The result was stable, controlled flight at as little as 32 knots, with corresponding huge changes in trim. Only one airframe was built, and it now lives at the Royal Air Force Museum at Cosford. We look at what the H.126 actually proved in flight test, how its data informed later powered-lift research from the Buccaneer wing to the C-17, why the technology never reached production, and what modern STOL designers still take from those Hunting wind tunnel runs.
