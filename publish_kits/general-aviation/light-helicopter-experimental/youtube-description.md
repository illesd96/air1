The US Army initiated the Light Helicopter Experimental program in 1982 with a brutal-sounding goal: replace both the AH-1 Cobra and the OH-58 Kiowa with a single, stealthy, networked reconnaissance and light attack helicopter. The program ran a long competition through the late 1980s before selecting the Boeing-Sikorsky team's RAH-66 Comanche in 1991. The Comanche prototype first flew on 4 January 1996 and matured into a remarkable airframe: five-blade composite rotor, fenestron tail, faceted radar cross-section, and an internal weapons bay. The Army accepted two prototypes. Then in February 2004, after Iraq-era spending pressures and a quiet acknowledgement that low-observable scout helicopters were not actually what the Army needed, the Comanche was cancelled. The total LHX program had consumed close to 7 billion dollars over two decades. We walk through how LHX was structured, why the Army really thought stealth helicopters would survive in 2004, what the Comanche prototypes proved, and how LHX's cancellation reshaped Army aviation procurement.
