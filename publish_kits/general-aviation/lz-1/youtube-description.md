On 2 July 1900, Count Ferdinand von Zeppelin's LZ 1 was towed out of a floating hangar moored on Lake Constance, southern Germany. Its aluminum girder frame held 17 separate hydrogen gas cells, two 14 horsepower Daimler petrol engines on outboard nacelles, and a fragile control system that barely answered the helm. It flew for about 18 minutes that first day, climbed to roughly 410 meters, and demonstrated for the first time that a rigid lighter-than-air vehicle could be controlled in level flight. It flew only three times before investors lost confidence and the airship was scrapped. We trace the technical decisions that became the Zeppelin design language: longitudinal aluminum frame, internal gas cells, paired engines, lake-launched hangar, and how LZ 1 led directly to the great rigids of the next four decades.
