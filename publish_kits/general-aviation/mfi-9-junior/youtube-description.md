Bjorn Andreasson drew his BA-7 in 1958 as a personal sport plane. Malmo Flygindustri picked up the design and put it into production in Sweden as the MFI-9 Junior, then licensed it to Bolkow in West Germany where over 300 were built as the Bo 208. It was clean, all-metal, low-wing, two-seat, fun. It should have stayed a sport plane. Instead, in May 1969, Swedish nobleman Count Carl Gustaf von Rosen acquired five MFI-9Bs through a Tanzanian intermediary, mounted Matra rocket pods under the wings, and flew them into Biafra to support the secessionists in the Nigerian Civil War. The unarmed sportplanes attacked Nigerian airfields and Shell-BP oil installations, knocking out a substantial portion of the Nigerian Air Force on the ground in late 1969. The episode is now known as the Biafran Babies operation and remains one of the strangest combat employments of a civil sport plane in history. We tell that story, look at what the airframe was really designed for, how it compares to its contemporary Bolkow Junior, and what surviving MFI-9s are doing today.
