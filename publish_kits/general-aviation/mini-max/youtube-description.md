When Wayne Ison drew the Mini-MAX in 1984 he had three numbers on the napkin: minimum cost, minimum space, minimum skill. The result was a single-seat, mid-wing, strut-braced wooden ultralight an absolute beginner could finish in a one-car garage with hand tools. The original TEAM Mini-MAX cleared Part 103, weighed under 254 pounds empty in its lightest trim, and flew behind a two-stroke Rotax that fit in the trunk of a sedan. Over the decades the family grew into the Hi-MAX, V-MAX, Z-MAX and the larger experimental AeroMax, all with the same builder-friendly DNA. Today, with TEAM Aircraft of Bradyville, Tennessee still shipping plans and kits, the Mini-MAX has become a quiet pillar of the American grassroots ultralight community. We walk through what the airframe is really like to build, how the wood-and-fabric structure compares to the modern composite kits, what powerplant choices actually work, and why the Mini-MAX keeps winning over first-time builders four decades after first flight.
