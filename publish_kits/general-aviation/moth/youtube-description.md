In 1925 Geoffrey de Havilland produced the DH.60 Moth and changed everything. Until then a private aeroplane meant a war-surplus Avro 504 and a barn full of mechanics. The Moth offered folding wings, a four-cylinder Cirrus, and a price the new aero clubs could actually meet. By the early 1930s every light aircraft flying in Britain was nicknamed a Moth, whether de Havilland built it or not. The family grew into the Gipsy Moth, the cabin Puss and Leopard Moths, the racy Hornet, the Fox, the four-seat Dragonfly, and the immortal Tiger Moth that would train the wartime Commonwealth. We trace the lineage from Stag Lane to Hatfield, look at how the Gipsy engine family made the Moths economical, and visit the British and Australian collections that still keep them airworthy. If you have flown a Moth, the comments are yours.
