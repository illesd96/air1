By 1947 Piper had a problem. The J-3 Cub had sold over 19,000 copies but its open-cowl, exposed fuel tank between the pilot's knees and 65-horsepower Continental no longer matched what postwar buyers wanted. The PA-11 Cub Special was the answer. The fuel tank moved into the left wing root. The cowl wrapped fully around a 90-horsepower Continental C-90. The pilot soloed from the front seat for the first time on a production Cub. The airframe was unmistakable but the cockpit suddenly felt like a real aircraft. Piper built around 1,500 PA-11s between 1947 and 1949 before the design evolved into the PA-18 Super Cub that would conquer Alaska. Today the PA-11 sits in a sweet spot of the Cub collector market: more capable than a J-3, less common than a Super Cub, and rare enough to draw a crowd at any vintage fly-in. We walk through the engineering changes that distinguish a PA-11 from a J-3, what to inspect on an original C-90, and why so many owners insist this is the best Cub Piper ever built.
