The North American Mustang I, originally built for the RAF, had a stunning low-altitude profile thanks to its Allison V-1710 but ran out of breath above 15,000 feet. In April 1942 Rolls-Royce engineer Ronnie Harker test flew an early Mustang at Duxford, recommended an immediate Merlin conversion, and within months Rolls-Royce had wedged five Merlin 65s into five Mustang I airframes as the Mustang Mk.X. The resulting performance, with a critical altitude shoved into the high 20s of thousands of feet and top speeds north of 400 mph, was so transformative that North American immediately adopted the Packard-built Merlin V-1650 as the basis for the production P-51B. The legend of the Mustang as long-range escort fighter starts here. We trace the Harker test flight, the engineering compromises Rolls had to make in a tight cowling, what survived from the Mk.X experiments, and how this remains a textbook case of right engine, right airframe, right moment.
