On a farm field at Brandis in 1944, Arthur Sack pushed a literal disc-winged airplane into the wind. The Sack AS-6 was the culmination of a 1939 model competition idea that had grown, in five iterations, into a full-scale aircraft cobbled together from Bf 109 cockpit and undercarriage components, an Argus engine, and a near-circular wooden wing. Test pilot Rolf Baltabol could not coax it into sustained flight. It made a few hops, suffered undercarriage damage, and was eventually destroyed by Allied attacks in 1945. We separate what the historical record actually says from the postwar flying saucer mythology that latched onto it, what Sack got right aerodynamically, and where his disc-wing idea reappears in later experiments like the Vought XF5U. No occult pictures, no Haunebu nonsense, just the strange real story.
