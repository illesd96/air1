The Stinson 108 rolled out of the factory in 1946 with a simple promise: bring the family flying. Developed from the prewar Model 10A Voyager, it offered four seats, a 150 to 165 horsepower Franklin engine, and the kind of honest taildragger manners that turned demobilized airmen into civilian pilots. Consolidated Vultee built more than 5,000 of them between 1946 and 1950 before the postwar light aircraft market collapsed. The fabric-covered wings and steel tube fuselage made it forgiving to fly and, decades later, achievable to restore in a home hangar. Today the 108 has one of the most loyal owner communities in general aviation, with parts support from cottage suppliers and an annual fly-in that still fills a grass strip. We look at why the Voyager, Station Wagon and Flying Station Wagon variants each found a niche, how the Franklin engine drama nearly killed parts support, and what makes a good 108 airframe worth pursuing in 2026.
