In 1932, Italian engineer Luigi Stipa flew the result of his intubed-propeller theory: an aircraft whose entire fuselage was a single ducted fan with a 120 horsepower de Havilland Gipsy engine and propeller mounted inside it. Caproni built the prototype. It flew on 7 October 1932. Performance gains were modest but real, and stability in level flight surprised the test pilots. The Regia Aeronautica was not interested, and Stipa's idea was left on the shelf, although he claimed for the rest of his life that turbojet and turbofan engineers had borrowed his work. We look at the actual physics of the ducted-fan fuselage, what the flight reports recorded, where Stipa's theory shows up in modern aviation, and the modern Australian and Italian replica builds that proved the original wasn't a hoax. Strange beauty, real engineering.
