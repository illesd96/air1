Stoddard-Hamilton's Glasair line started in 1979 in Arlington, Washington as one of the first wet-layup composite kit aircraft for amateur builders. The Glasair III, introduced in the late 1980s, was the line's high-water mark: a 300 horsepower Lycoming IO-540, retractable gear, a sliding bubble canopy, and a published top speed that punched into the upper 200s of knots. Done right, a III can keep up with a Mooney Ovation while burning Lycoming-not-Continental fuel. Done badly, it punishes a builder who shortcuts the layup. We look at the III's lineage from Tom Hamilton's first Glasair, the differences between the early SH-3 and the late T-tail, the Advanced Aero Components purchase of assets in 2017, and what the EAA community thinks separates a good airframe from one to walk away from.
