In 1989 Sukhoi and Gulfstream announced what would have been the first post-Cold-War supersonic business jet, the S-21. The proposal targeted Mach 2 cruise, transatlantic range, and a roughly six to ten passenger cabin. It went through repeated configuration changes, considered a variety of engines including Rolls-Royce Tay variants and Soloviev D-30s, and ultimately failed to attract launch customers willing to underwrite the supersonic certification and emissions risks. The partnership wound down quietly in the mid-1990s. We trace the program's birth, the political environment that made it briefly imaginable, the airframe and engine trades that were never resolved, and the lessons that modern attempts at civil supersonic flight from Aerion to Boom are still being forced to relearn. A document of post-Cold-War aerospace optimism.
