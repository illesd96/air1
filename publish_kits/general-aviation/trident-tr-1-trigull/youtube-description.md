Trident Aircraft was founded in 1971 in Burnaby, British Columbia to bring back the family amphibian after the Republic Seabee went out of production. The TR-1 Trigull was the result: a five-seat, single-engine, high-wing amphibious flying boat with retractable tricycle gear and a 300-horsepower Lycoming IO-540 mounted high above the cabin in pusher configuration. The first prototype flew in 1973. Performance was reasonable and the cabin was actually comfortable, both of which the Seabee had struggled with. Trident moved operations to Sidney on Vancouver Island, built two more prototypes, and pushed for Canadian type certification through the 1970s. Then the bills came due. Certification took longer and cost more than the company's investors had projected, no large buyer emerged, and Trident folded with only three TR-1 airframes ever completed. None survive in flying condition. We trace the Trigull's design choices, the certification arc that broke the company, what the British Columbia floatplane community remembers, and what a modern Trigull-class amphibian might look like.
