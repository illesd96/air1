Ultralight aviation in the United States is governed by FAA Part 103, a 1982 regulation that defines a class of aircraft with no pilot certificate or medical required — provided the aircraft weighs 254 pounds or less, carries one person, holds 5 gallons of fuel or less, stalls at 24 knots or lower and tops out at 55 knots. Around the world, similar 'microlight' categories exist with their own quirks.

In this video: what Part 103 actually says, why it was written, the real safety record of single-seat ultralights, the difference between a true ultralight and a Light Sport Aircraft (and why the difference matters legally), and what kind of training serious ultralight pilots actually pursue — even though none is required.
