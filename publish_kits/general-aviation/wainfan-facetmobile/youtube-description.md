Barnaby Wainfan spends his day job as a senior aerodynamicist at Northrop Grumman and his off hours writing the Wind Tunnel column in Kitplanes magazine. Somewhere in between he drew and built one of the most original homebuilt aircraft ever flown: the FMX-4 Facetmobile. The airframe is a low-aspect-ratio lifting body with no conventional wings, the upper and lower surfaces made up of flat faceted panels in the same family of shapes you would recognise from the F-117 Nighthawk. Wainfan first flew the Facetmobile in 1993 and has flown it on and off ever since, demonstrating that a faceted lifting body can be docile, stable and surprisingly efficient at low speeds. We look at the aerodynamic case for low aspect ratio lifting bodies, why Wainfan picked facets rather than smooth curves, what the Facetmobile's flight test campaign actually proved, and how the design has influenced both modern UAV planforms and other homebuilt aerodynamicists. Aerodynamics nerds welcome in the comments.
