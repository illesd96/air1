The Perlan Project set out to prove that polar stratospheric mountain waves could carry a glider above 90,000 feet, and Greg Cole of Windward Performance was asked to build the aircraft that could do it. The Perlan 2 is the result: a pressurized, two-seat tandem sailplane with an 84-foot composite wing optimized for thin air, a sealed cabin running its own oxygen supply, and a glide ratio over 40 to 1. Airbus joined as title sponsor. The flight test program runs out of El Calafate, Patagonia, where the Andes generate the world's most reliable stratospheric wave. On 2 September 2018 the Perlan 2 reached 76,124 feet, breaking the absolute altitude record for level wing-borne flight outside of the rocket-powered SR-71 and U-2 family. We look at how the wing is shaped, why the cabin pressure schedule is unlike anything else in gliding, what the data is telling atmospheric scientists about ozone and stratospheric mixing, and where the program goes next.
