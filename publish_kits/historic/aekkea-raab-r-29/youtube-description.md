The AEKKEA-RAAB R-29 first flew in 1936 as Greece's homegrown attempt at a domestic single-seat parasol monoplane, intended both as a basic trainer and a light fighter. Designed by exiled German engineer Antonius Raab in partnership with Greek industrialists at the AEKKEA workshops near Athens, only a handful were ever built before the Italian and German invasions of 1940 to 1941 destroyed Greek aviation infrastructure. In this video we examine the political situation that drove Greece toward domestic production, why a parasol wing was hopelessly obsolete by 1936, how Raab ended up in Athens after leaving the Raab-Katzenstein firm in Germany, and what the R-29's lineage tells us about technology transfer between small nations and dispossessed European engineers. We also clarify the persistent confusion between this aircraft and the unrelated Raab-Katzenstein RK series.
