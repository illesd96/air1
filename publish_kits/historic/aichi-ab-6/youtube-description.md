The Aichi AB-6 (Experimental 7-Shi Reconnaissance Seaplane) was a single-engined three-seat biplane floatplane built by Aichi in 1933 as their submission to the Imperial Japanese Navy's 7-Shi (1932-fiscal-year) reconnaissance seaplane competition. The AB-6 featured twin main floats, an open cockpit arrangement for pilot, observer and gunner, and used a radial engine of moderate output. Only a single prototype was completed. The competition was won by the Kawanishi E7K, which entered widespread IJN service through the 1930s and saw early-war combat. The AB-6 prototype was scrapped, and Aichi pivoted toward dive bombers, eventually producing the D3A Val. In this video we examine the 7-Shi specification, Aichi's design philosophy versus Kawanishi's, what specific aerodynamic and performance weaknesses doomed the AB-6, and how losing this competition pushed Aichi away from reconnaissance work and toward the strike role they would dominate at Pearl Harbor. We also untangle the Japanese 'Shi' designation system that confuses Western readers.
