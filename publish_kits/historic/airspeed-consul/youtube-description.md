The Airspeed Consul tells one of the strangest stories in postwar British aviation. It was not a clean-sheet design — it was a 1946 conversion of the wartime Airspeed Oxford trainer, with the gun turret removed and a cabin door cut into the side. Airspeed Limited had been co-founded in 1931 by the aeronautical engineer A. Hessell Tiltman and the novelist Nevil Shute Norway, who served as a director until 1938. By the time the Consul flew, Shute had left the firm, but the company that built the aircraft was effectively his legacy.

The Consul filled a curious gap: too small to be a real airliner, too big for private use. Air-taxi firms, photographic survey companies, and embryonic colonial airlines bought it in over 30 countries. By the mid-1950s the de Havilland Dove had eaten its market. By 1960, almost every Consul had been scrapped. One airworthy example survives today.

This video covers the conversion process, the Nevil Shute connection, the colonial operators, and what little remains.
