The ANBO V is one of aviation's quiet ghosts. Designed by Antanas Gustaitis for the Lithuanian Army in 1931, this parasol-wing monoplane trainer represents something unusual: a small, newly independent nation building its own combat-capable training fleet in a workshop on the edge of Europe. A developed version, the ANBO 51, followed in 1936 and 1938, used to introduce student pilots to the demands of military flying. The aircraft never fought a famous battle. It never broke a speed record. But it is a fingerprint of Lithuanian national ambition between the wars, and most of its surviving examples vanished with Soviet occupation in 1940. In this video we walk through the design choices that made the ANBO V suitable for a small country with limited industry, the engineer who built a national aviation program nearly single-handed, and what happened to the airframes after independence ended.
