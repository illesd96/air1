The Armstrong Whitworth F.K.10 was a British two-seat quadruplane fighter aircraft built during the First World War. Designed by Frederik Koolhoven — the Dutch engineer who would later run his own aircraft company in the Netherlands — it was one of the very few quadruplane aircraft ever to reach actual production status.

The idea behind quadruplane configurations was simple: more wing area in a more compact span, theoretically giving better climb and manoeuvre performance. In practice, the four-wing layout produced complicated interference drag, poor pilot visibility, and a structure that was hard to keep rigged correctly. The Sopwith Triplane had briefly proved that more than two wings could work in 1916-17, but four wings turned out to be a step too far.

The RFC and RNAS ordered small numbers of F.K.10s. None were used operationally. The type was effectively quietly retired.

This video covers Koolhoven's British career, the quadruplane reasoning, the F.K.10 design, and why the four-wing fighter concept died with this aircraft.
