The Avro Type H, designated 501 and 503, is a piece of pre-war British aviation that almost no one outside specialist circles can name. Built in 1913 as an early military seaplane, it was originally conceived as an amphibian, with a prototype that mounted a single large main float under the fuselage and two tiny outriggers under the wings. The configuration was a bet on stability through ballast — and it didn't really pay off. Production variants moved to twin floats. The aircraft was used by the Royal Naval Air Service for training and reconnaissance in the months before and during the opening of the First World War, and informed every later British naval design.
