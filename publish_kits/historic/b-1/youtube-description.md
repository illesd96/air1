The Boeing B-1 first flew on December 27, 1919, a single-engine wooden biplane flying boat designed by William Boeing himself in the lean years after WWI. With Boeing's military contracts evaporating, the company needed civil work to survive. On March 3, 1919, Boeing personally co-piloted a B-1 carrying 60 letters from Vancouver, BC, to Seattle, completing what is widely cited as the first international airmail flight in U.S. history. The B-1 carried two passengers behind the pilot, on top of the mail bag. Only one of the original B-1 was built, but the design seeded the Model 6/B-1D/B-1E family that operated into the 1930s. In this video we trace the desperate postwar pivot of the Boeing Company, untangle the messy naming (B-1 vs. Model 6 vs. BB-1), examine the Hall-Scott engine that powered it, and explain why the 'first international airmail' claim has fine print. The original B-1 survives today at the Museum of Flight in Seattle.
