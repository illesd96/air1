The Bartel BM-5 was Poland's first indigenous-designed advanced trainer, built at the Samolot factory in Poznań from around 1928 onwards. About 70 were completed, and the type carried the Polish Air Force through the entire interwar pilot pipeline — the same pipeline that produced the pilots of the famous No. 303 Squadron RAF.

Its designer, Ryszard Bartel, had worked at the German Albatros works during WWI before returning to newly-independent Poland — a reminder that many Polish interwar designers were trained in German aviation and would turn those skills against Germany in 1939. The BM-5 used the Wright Whirlwind J-5 radial — the same engine that powered Lindbergh's Spirit of St. Louis — because the new Polish republic could not yet build its own engines.

Almost every BM-5 was destroyed in September 1939. No complete original is known to survive. But the pilots who learned on it escaped through Romania, France, and finally Britain — where 303 Squadron, flying Hurricanes, became the highest-scoring RAF squadron of the Battle of Britain.

Sources: Polish Aviation Museum, RAF 303 Squadron history, Wikipedia.
