The Bernard 70 was a 1930 paper design by the Société des Avions Bernard for a single-seat low-wing monoplane fighter, intended for the French Air Force's contemporary fighter competition. The design was never built as a fighter. Instead, the lineage forked twice: first into the Bernard S-72 racing monoplane, designed to compete for the Coupe Deutsch de la Meurthe, and then refigured back into single-seat fighter prototypes designated Bernard 74-01 and 74-02. Only the two 74-series prototypes were ever built. Neither was procured. The Société des Avions Bernard, founded by Adolphe Bernard, struggled financially through the early 1930s and ultimately ceased operations by 1935. In this video we untangle the complicated 70 to S-72 to 74 lineage that confuses many French aviation reference works, examine what the Bernard team actually got right (clean low-wing aerodynamics), what they got wrong (no production-ready engine partnership), and how the failure of small interwar French firms like Bernard set the stage for the nationalization of French aviation in 1936. We also clarify the unrelated Bernard 80 and 190 designations.
