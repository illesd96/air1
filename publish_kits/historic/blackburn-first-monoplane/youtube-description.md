The Blackburn First Monoplane was the first aircraft built by Robert Blackburn, completed in 1909 at his workshop in Leeds, Yorkshire. It first attempted flight on May 24, 1910, at Marske-by-the-Sea, piloted by Robert Weiss. The attempt resulted in a crash that damaged the airframe; Blackburn declined to rebuild and abandoned the design. He learned from the failure and went on to design the Blackburn Mercury, Type D (the oldest airworthy British aircraft, still flying), and ultimately a long line of military aircraft including the Skua, Roc, Buccaneer, and Beverley. In this video we examine the parasol monoplane configuration borrowed from French pioneers, the specific structural and balance errors Blackburn made, why he chose the windswept Yorkshire coast at Marske for testing, and how the failure of one aircraft seeded an entire British industry. We also untangle the persistent confusion between the 'First Monoplane' and the 'Second Monoplane' (a different, more successful design).
