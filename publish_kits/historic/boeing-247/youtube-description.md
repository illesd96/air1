In 1932 Boeing's parent company also owned United Air Lines. So when Boeing built the revolutionary 247, United got all 60 of the first production run before anyone else could buy one. That includes TWA. Jack Frye at TWA had no choice - he called Donald Douglas in Santa Monica and asked him to build something better. Douglas built the DC-1. Then the DC-2. Then the DC-3. Boeing's first-mover advantage was destroyed inside three years by its own vertical integration.

The 247 was a genuinely brilliant aircraft - retractable gear, all-metal monocoque construction, autopilot, controllable-pitch propellers. It cut the New York to Chicago flight time by 25%. But it carried only 10 passengers, was outclassed by the DC-3's 21, and the antitrust breakup of United Aircraft and Transport in 1934 sealed its fate. One airworthy 247 still flies today in the Museum of Flight in Seattle.
