The Boeing Model 6D, also designated 6E, B-1D, and B-1E depending on engine and finish, was a small pusher-configuration biplane flying boat built by Boeing between 1928 and 1929. It evolved from the earlier B-1, but Boeing was already pivoting toward the Model 80 trimotor and the Model 40 mail planes; the 6D never found a serious market. Powered by Wright Whirlwind engines in pusher configuration, the design was already aerodynamically obsolete by the time it flew. In this video we untangle Boeing's chaotic interwar nomenclature, examine why pusher biplane flying boats were a dead end, look at the handful of operators (mostly private owners and small flying clubs in the Pacific Northwest), and explain how this kind of marginal product actually informed Boeing's later success: every failed model taught the company what NOT to build. We also clarify how the Model 6 lineage relates (and doesn't relate) to the original B-1.
