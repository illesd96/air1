The Boeing Model 7, also designated BB-1, first flew January 7, 1920, an evolution of the B-1 with a tighter cockpit arrangement: pilot in front and two passengers shoulder-to-shoulder directly behind. Powered by a Hall-Scott L-6 inline engine in tractor configuration, only one was built. The aircraft was never a commercial success, but it represents an essential transitional piece in Boeing's struggle to find civil markets after losing its WWI contracts. In this video we examine why the BB-1 designation appears in some sources and not others, look at how William Boeing personally test-flew the Model 7, trace the lineage from B-1 to Model 7 to the later Model 6 series, and explain the seating geometry that made the Model 7 nearly impossible to load. We also discuss why the Hall-Scott L-6 was a poor engine choice and how that influenced Boeing's later partnerships with Pratt and Whitney. Boeing's interwar story is often skipped to jump from the B-17 backwards; the Model 7 is the bone-dry middle.
