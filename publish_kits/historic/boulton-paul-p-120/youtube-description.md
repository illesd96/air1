First flown August 6, 1952, the Boulton Paul P.120 was a tailless delta-winged research aircraft built to ER.114T to investigate handling and aerodynamics for future British delta fighters. Derived from the earlier P.111, the P.120 featured a small swept fin, improved elevons, and was powered by a single Rolls-Royce Nene. On August 29, 1952, just 23 days after first flight, the aircraft suffered an elevon flutter incident, the tail unit detached, and test pilot Squadron Leader A.E. 'Ben' Gunn ejected successfully. The wreckage fell near Salisbury Plain. The aircraft was never rebuilt. It was the last aircraft Boulton Paul Aircraft Ltd ever flew under its own design. In this video we trace the British delta wing research effort, including the Avro 707, Fairey Delta 1, and how the P.120's data still contributed to the Gloster Javelin and beyond, the structural failure that ended its short life, and Boulton Paul's slow pivot to subsystems work. We also clarify the confusing P.111/P.120 nomenclature.
