The Braunschweig LF-1 Zaunkönig ('wren' in German) was a STOL single-seat light aircraft designed in 1939 by Professor Hermann Winter at the Technische Hochschule Braunschweig (now TU Braunschweig). Winter's brief was to build an aircraft so easy to fly that novice students could safely solo with minimal instruction; he achieved this through a combination of full-span leading-edge slats, generous flaps, a slow stalling speed, and stable static margins that resisted spin entry almost regardless of pilot input. First flown in December 1940, the original V-1 prototype was followed by an improved V-2 and V-3. Postwar, V-2 was shipped to Britain and registered G-ALUA, where it flew with the Ultra-Light Aircraft Association into the 1950s and beyond. In this video we examine the specific aerodynamic features that make the Zaunkönig forgiving, Hermann Winter's research methodology, why such an aircraft was funded under wartime Germany (training pilots cheaply was a strategic priority), and the Zaunkönig's lasting influence on STOL aircraft design from the Helio Courier to modern bush flyers. We also clarify why the aircraft is sometimes referenced as the 'Winter LF-1' or 'TUB LF-1' in different sources.
