On October 22, 1938, Italian test pilot Mario Pezzi piloted the Caproni Ca.161bis to 17,083 m (56,047 ft) over Montecelio, setting an FAI-recognized altitude record for piston-engined aircraft that still stands in 2026. The Ca.161 was a deliberately conservative biplane, derived from the Ca.113 trainer, fitted with a Piaggio P.XI RC.100/2v radial driving a four-bladed wooden propeller, all wrapped around a pressure suit adapted from Italian Navy diving rigs. In this video we examine why Caproni chose a biplane (lift at low density), how the pressure suit nearly suffocated Pezzi on descent, why the open cockpit was a calculated weight trade, and the geopolitics of why fascist Italy fought Britain and Germany for stratospheric records. We also clarify the often-confused difference between piston, jet, and balloon altitude records.
