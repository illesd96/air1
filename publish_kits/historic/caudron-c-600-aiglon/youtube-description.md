The Caudron C.600 Aiglon (Eaglet) is, in some ways, the perfect interwar French aircraft — small, wooden, elegantly proportioned, powered by a tiny Renault inverted inline, and almost completely forgotten today.

First flown in 1935, the Aiglon was Caudron-Renault's bid for the French touring and sport market, sitting in the same niche the de Havilland Moth occupied in Britain. It competed in air races, set a few minor distance records, and was sold to private owners and aero clubs across France. The German invasion of 1940 ended its civilian career, and most surviving airframes were either pressed into Luftwaffe communications duties or quietly scrapped.

In this video we look at the Caudron-Renault partnership, how the Aiglon compared to its British and German contemporaries, what happened to the type during and after the occupation, and the handful of survivors that have been restored to flying condition.
