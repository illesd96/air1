The Curtiss-Bleecker SX-5-1 Helicopter was an American experimental rotary-wing aircraft built in 1926 to a patent by Maitland Bleecker. The concept was radical: instead of driving the main rotor directly, a single central engine drove four small tractor propellers, one mounted on the outboard end of each rotor blade, via internal shafts. The tip-mounted propellers would pull the blades around, generating lift through a complex arrangement Bleecker believed eliminated torque problems. In practice, the SX-5-1 vibrated catastrophically, struggled to lift its own weight, and was abandoned without ever achieving sustained free flight. Curtiss funded the project as a hedge bet during the early helicopter race. In this video we examine why tip-driven rotor concepts keep recurring (the Doblhoff WNF 342, the Hiller Hornet, the Saunders-Roe Skeeter) and keep failing, the specific structural and aerodynamic problems Bleecker encountered, and how Sikorsky's later success with conventional shaft drive validated the path Bleecker rejected. The Curtiss-Bleecker is preserved at the National Air and Space Museum's Steven F. Udvar-Hazy Center.
