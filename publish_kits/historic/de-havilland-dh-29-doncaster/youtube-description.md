The de Havilland DH.29 Doncaster was a British long-range high-wing monoplane of the 1920s, built by de Havilland to an Air Ministry specification for comparative trials. Two examples were constructed. The contract was driven by a specific question: could a cantilever monoplane offer significantly better long-range performance than a contemporary biplane of equivalent power? The Doncaster's trials helped answer that question in the affirmative, even though the aircraft itself was not adopted for production. The configuration was novel for Britain in 1921 — high cantilever wooden wing, internal cabin for crew and a small passenger or fuel load — and effectively previewed where civil aviation would go fifteen years later.
