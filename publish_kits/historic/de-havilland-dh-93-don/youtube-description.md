The de Havilland DH.93 Don is one of the most quietly embarrassing chapters in pre-war RAF procurement. Designed to Specification T.6/36 and intended to be a 'universal' trainer covering pilot training, navigation, gunnery, and wireless instruction, it first flew in June 1937. The RAF ordered 250.

In service the Don was a disappointment. The 525-hp de Havilland Gypsy King engine was underpowered for the aircraft's weight, performance was mediocre, and the rear gun turret originally specified was deleted before service entry. Of the 50 actually delivered, most were used as communications aircraft or ground instructional airframes — and the remaining order was cancelled. By 1939, the Airspeed Oxford had completely taken over the multi-engine training role.

This video covers the original specification, the design choices that went wrong, the cancellation, and what the Don story tells us about how the pre-war RAF made and unmade aircraft contracts. We also avoid the popular myth that the type was 'a death trap' — the actual record was disappointing performance, not catastrophic safety.
