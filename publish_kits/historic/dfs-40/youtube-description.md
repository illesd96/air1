The DFS 40, originally designated Lippisch Delta V, first flew in 1939 at the Deutsche Forschungsanstalt für Segelflug (DFS), the German Research Institute for Sailplane Flight. It was Alexander Lippisch's most advanced tailless research aircraft to date, a refinement of the earlier DFS 39 (Delta IV), and intended to directly inform what would become the Me 163 Komet rocket-powered interceptor. The DFS 40 was conventionally powered by a piston engine, used to validate the aerodynamics in isolation from the volatile Walter rocket propellants. Only one prototype was completed. In this video we trace the entire Lippisch tailless lineage from the 1928 Ente through the Delta I through V, examine the wind-tunnel data that justified the layout, look at how Heinkel and later Messerschmitt took over the work, and explain why a 'tailless' aircraft is not the same as a 'flying wing.' We also clarify why the DFS 40 is so rarely mentioned in popular Me 163 narratives, the war erased most documentation, and Lippisch himself avoided talking about specific airframes postwar.
