On 17 December 1903 at Kitty Hawk, North Carolina, the Wright Brothers' first powered aircraft completed four flights. The longest was 59 seconds, covering 852 feet (260 m). Later that same day, a gust flipped the aircraft on the ground and damaged it beyond repair. It never flew again.

In this video we work through what actually happened, what didn't, and what the Wrights did next. We address the most persistent myths: that nobody saw the flight (there were five witnesses), that the world immediately accepted it (in fact, much of European aviation refused to believe the Wrights until Wilbur's 1908 demonstrations in France), and that the Flyer was the basis for further development (it wasn't — the Flyer II and Flyer III of 1904-05 were almost completely different aircraft).

We also cover what happened to the airframe itself: it spent years in storage, was nearly lost in a 1913 flood, then loaned to the Science Museum in London after a long dispute with the Smithsonian, and finally returned to Washington in 1948.

Sources: Smithsonian National Air and Space Museum, Wright Brothers National Memorial, McCullough biography, Wikipedia.
