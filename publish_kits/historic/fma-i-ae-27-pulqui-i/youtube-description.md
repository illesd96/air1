On August 9, 1947, the FMA I.Ae. 27 Pulqui ('arrow' in Mapudungun) made its first flight at Cordoba, Argentina, becoming the first jet-powered aircraft designed and built in Latin America. The team was led by Émile Dewoitine, the French designer of the D.520, who had fled France postwar due to Vichy collaboration accusations. Powered by a single Rolls-Royce Derwent, the Pulqui I exhibited poor stability and was abandoned after limited testing. Dewoitine departed, and Kurt Tank (designer of the Fw 190) arrived to lead the unrelated Pulqui II program. In this video we untangle the Pulqui I from the Pulqui II, examine the Peronist industrial policy that funded both, look at why Argentina chose to recruit war-tainted European designers, and explain the aerodynamic limitations of the I.Ae. 27 that doomed it. We also bust the common claim that the Pulqui II was a redesigned Pulqui I, they share almost nothing.
