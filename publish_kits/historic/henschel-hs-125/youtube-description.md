First flown April 30, 1934, the Henschel Hs 125 was a single-engined, low-wing monoplane advanced trainer prototype built by Henschel und Sohn AG in Schönefeld. Only two prototypes were completed. The Hs 125 V1 had a fixed undercarriage and the V2 introduced refinements, but neither could overcome the Reichsluftfahrtministerium's preference for the Arado Ar 96. Henschel pivoted hard, focusing on the Hs 126 reconnaissance aircraft and the Hs 129 ground attack aircraft, plus their core tank business (Tiger I, Panther). The Hs 125 designation is, of course, much better known today as the Hawker Siddeley HS.125 business jet, an entirely unrelated British design from 1962. In this video we examine the Hs 125's aerodynamic conservatism, the Luftwaffe's 1934 trainer requirements, why Henschel's aviation work pivoted toward attack aircraft rather than fighters or bombers, and the naming coincidence with the British jet. We also dig into the fragmentary surviving documentation, as wartime Schönefeld was heavily bombed and most prewar prototype data was destroyed.
