The Junkers K 37 was the Swedish-built military designation for what Junkers in Germany called the S 36. First flown September 5, 1927, it was a twin-engined all-metal low-wing monoplane, ostensibly a mail and light transport aircraft, but configured at AB Flygindustri in Limhamn, Sweden, as a bomber with defensive armament and bomb racks. The Treaty of Versailles forbade Junkers from building military aircraft inside Germany, so the Swedish subsidiary became the workaround. A single K 37 was sold to Japan in 1928, where Mitsubishi reverse-engineered it as the Mitsubishi Ki-2 light bomber, which served the Imperial Japanese Army Air Force through the 1930s. In this video we trace the Versailles-era trickery: AB Flygindustri's status, the financial backers, the routing of K 37 parts to and from Limhamn, and the technical lineage from S 36 to K 37 to Ki-2. We also dispel the lazy claim that the Ki-2 was a Junkers Ju 86, the two are different generations and different parents.
