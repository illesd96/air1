The Langley Aerodrome is a pioneering but unsuccessful manned, tandem-wing-configuration powered flying machine designed at the close of the 19th century by Smithsonian Institution Secretary Samuel Pierpont Langley. The U.S. Army paid $50,000 for the project in 1898 after Langley's successful flights with small-scale unmanned models — the Aerodrome No. 5 and No. 6 — two years earlier. The full-scale manned Aerodrome was launched twice by catapult from a houseboat on the Potomac River, on 7 October and 8 December 1903, with Charles Manly piloting both attempts. Both ended in immediate crashes into the water; the structural design failed under the catapult launch loads, and no actual flight occurred. Nine days after the second failure, the Wright Brothers flew at Kitty Hawk. In a controversial 1914 trial, Glenn Curtiss flew a modified version of the Aerodrome and the Smithsonian briefly claimed Langley as the first to have built a capable flying machine — a claim later retracted.
