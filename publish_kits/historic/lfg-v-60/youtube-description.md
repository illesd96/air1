The LFG V 60 was a small, single-engine, tandem-seat floatplane training aircraft built in Germany in the mid-1920s by Luft-Fahrzeug-Gesellschaft, a firm whose name barely outlived the decade. About five aircraft were constructed. Under the Treaty of Versailles, Germany was prohibited from operating combat aircraft, but training airframes — particularly those that could double as civil sport machines — fell into a legal gray zone that the German aviation industry exploited rigorously throughout the Weimar period. The V 60 is a small artifact of that environment. In this video we look at the structure of the aircraft, the role floatplane trainers played in the secret pilot pipeline that Germany was already quietly building by the late 1920s, and why nearly all evidence of LFG's design work has been lost or scattered into a handful of footnotes. We rely on Wikipedia's LFG V 60 entry and standard German interwar aviation references.
