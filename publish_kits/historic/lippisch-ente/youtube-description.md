On June 11, 1928, at the Wasserkuppe in Germany, pilot Fritz Stamer cast off in the Lippisch Ente (German for 'duck'), a tailless canard glider with two Sander black-powder rockets clamped to the rear fuselage. He fired them sequentially. The Ente flew about 1,500 m at 90 km/h, then caught fire on the third test and was destroyed. It was the world's first manned, rocket-powered, heavier-than-air flight, beating every other claimant by a decade. This video traces how Alexander Lippisch's obsession with tailless flight led to the Ente, then the DFS 39, then the Delta IV, then the Me 163 Komet. We also tackle the persistent myth that the Ente was 'just a stunt' (Friedrich Stamer's flight logs say otherwise) and explain why canard layout was crucial when you can't trust your engine not to blow up.
