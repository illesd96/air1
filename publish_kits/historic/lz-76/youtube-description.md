Zeppelin LZ 76, military designation L 33, was a Type R 'Super Zeppelin' commissioned September 9, 1916. On the night of September 23-24, 1916, she bombed London as part of a multi-airship raid. Hit by anti-aircraft fire over the East End and pursued by a B.E.2c flown by Lt. Alfred de Bathe Brandon, she lost too much hydrogen to return home and was set down by her commander Kapitänleutnant Alois Böcker near Little Wigborough, Essex. The crew tried to burn her, succeeded only partially, and surrendered to a local police constable. The Royal Navy salvaged the structural details, frames, gas-cell arrangement, fin geometry, and used them as the direct basis for the British R33 and R34 rigid airships. R34 went on to complete the first east-to-west transatlantic crossing in 1919. In this video we examine the night raid itself, the engagement that crippled LZ 76, Böcker's controversial decision to land in enemy territory rather than ditch in the North Sea, the Admiralty's careful disassembly and measurement, and the direct lineage to R33/R34. We also clarify the dual designation LZ 76 vs. L 33 vs. the unrelated Naval L 13.
