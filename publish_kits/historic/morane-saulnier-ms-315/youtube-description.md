The Morane-Saulnier MS.315 is one of those aircraft that history almost completely forgot — a French parasol-wing primary trainer that first flew in 1932, served the Armee de l'Air through the entire interwar period, survived the German occupation, and was still on French civilian registers decades after the war.

In this video we look at why France stayed loyal to the high-wing primary trainer formula long after most of Europe had moved on, how the MS.315 fit into the wider Morane-Saulnier line (a company that had once built Roland Garros' famous fighter), and what happened to the airframes that survived 1940. We also untangle the engine-swap variants — the original Salmson 9Nc radial gave way to a postwar Continental conversion that kept many MS.315s flying into the 1960s.

This is not a glamorous warbird story. It's the story of a teaching aircraft — and a reminder that for every Spitfire and Bf 109, dozens of obscure trainers did the unromantic work of turning teenagers into pilots.
