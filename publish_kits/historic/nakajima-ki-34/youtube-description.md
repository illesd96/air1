The Nakajima Ki-34 first flew on September 12, 1936, a twin-engined, all-metal low-wing monoplane with retractable gear, intended as a modern light transport for the Imperial Japanese Army. Its lineage runs directly through the Nakajima AT-2, itself derived from study of the Northrop 5A. The Allies later assigned it the reporting name 'Thora.' In this video we trace the technology transfer, intentional and otherwise, from American Douglas/Northrop monoplane design to Japan, explain why the Ki-34 was used for paratroop operations over the Dutch East Indies, and examine why only around 350 were built before being eclipsed by the Mitsubishi Ki-57 and Showa L2D (the licensed DC-3). We also bust the lazy framing that pre-war Japanese aviation was 'just copying' the West.
