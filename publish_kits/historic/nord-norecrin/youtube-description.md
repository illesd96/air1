The Nord 1200 Norécrin is the postwar aircraft Europe loved and America never knew about. It first flew on December 15, 1945 - the same week the United Nations was being created in London. Nord Aviation needed a civil light aircraft to keep its design teams employed after the German occupation ended, and the Norécrin was the answer: low-wing two- or three-seat cabin monoplane, retractable gear, all-metal construction, around 360 built.

It was a sophisticated little aircraft for 1945 - more so than the simultaneously-developed Piper PA-16. But it never reached the US market. Currency controls, the dominance of American manufacturers, and France's own protected market kept the Norécrin a European secret. About a dozen still fly today, mostly in France. We look at the design choices that made it good, and the trade barriers that kept it small.
