The Norge was a semi-rigid Italian-built airship designed by Umberto Nobile and bought by a consortium led by the Norwegian polar explorer Roald Amundsen and the American sponsor Lincoln Ellsworth. On 11-14 May 1926, the airship made the first verified trip of any kind to the North Pole, flying from Spitsbergen, over the Pole, and on to Alaska.

This was the first aircraft to fly over the polar ice cap between Europe and America. The 1926 flight also conclusively settled — at the time — competing American claims to have reached the pole earlier; later analysis would cast considerable doubt on Frederick Cook's 1908 claim and on Robert Peary's 1909 claim, and historians today generally credit the Norge crew with the first verified pole transit.

The story does not end well. Nobile returned to the Arctic in 1928 with the airship Italia, which crashed on the return from the pole. Amundsen led a rescue flight and was lost. The Italia survivors were eventually rescued by the Soviet icebreaker Krasin. Amundsen's body has never been found.
