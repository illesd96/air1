The Piper PA-16 Clipper is one of aviation's quiet survival stories. In 1947 Piper Aircraft was nearly bankrupt - the predicted postwar boom in private flying had failed to materialize, and the company was sitting on tooling for the J-3 Cub and its short-wing cousins with nothing to sell.

William Piper's engineers stretched the two-seat PA-15 Vagabond fuselage to seat four, kept the same wing, same tail, same 115hp Lycoming O-235. The PA-16 Clipper launched in 1949 at $2,295 - cheap enough that flying clubs could afford one. Just 736 were built before Pan American World Airways sued over the 'Clipper' trademark and Piper renamed the line. The PA-16's reused-parts engineering kept Piper alive long enough to develop the PA-18 Super Cub and PA-22 Tri-Pacer, both of which became iconic American light aircraft.
