The Potez X was a French 1920s general-purpose colonial transport aircraft designed and built by Potez, the firm founded by Henry Potez at Méaulte. The aircraft was intended for use across the French colonial network — North Africa, the Sahel, French Indochina — where airfield infrastructure was minimal and a single airframe might be expected to carry passengers, mail, light freight, and occasionally military supplies on the same week. The Potez X was a biplane with a robust structure designed for rough-strip operation. Production was small and the type was rapidly superseded by larger, all-metal designs in the late 1920s. In this video we walk through what early colonial aviation actually required from an airframe — the kind of strips, fuel logistics, and maintenance environment it had to survive — how Potez's design met that, and why the Potez X is best understood as a direct ancestor of the bush-plane lineage.
