After 1945, Germany was forbidden to build or fly powered aircraft. By 1951 the restrictions were starting to loosen, and the country quietly returned to the activity it had effectively invented in the 1920s: gliding. The Schleicher Ka-4 Rhönlerche II — designed by Rudolf Kaiser and first flown on 7 December 1953 — became the trainer that put Germans back in the air.

This video covers how the high-wing strut-braced layout was chosen for forgiving low-speed handling, how the Rhön gliding club tradition shaped the design philosophy, and why Schleicher's two-seat trainers came to dominate European clubs through the 1960s. We also look at how many surviving examples are still flying in Germany today — and why this aircraft is a quiet but important step in the history of the Federal Republic itself.

Sources: Schleicher company history, Deutscher Aero Club records, Wikipedia.
