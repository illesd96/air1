The Spartan 7W Executive is the closest thing the 1930s had to a corporate aircraft. Built by the Spartan Aircraft Company in Tulsa, Oklahoma — a city flush with oil money and ambition — the Executive was an all-metal, retractable-gear cabin monoplane with a 450-hp Pratt & Whitney Wasp Junior radial that gave it cruise speeds in the 200-mph range. First flown in 1936, it cost roughly the same as a Beechcraft Staggerwing but offered a totally different proposition: not a sport biplane, but a polished aluminum executive transport.

Only 34 were built before war production took over. Owners included oilmen, Hollywood pilots, and a handful of foreign governments. After 1941, most surviving Executives were impressed into the US Army Air Forces as UC-71s for liaison and staff transport. Postwar, a remarkable number ended up in private collections — and roughly half of the original production run is still flyable today, an extraordinary survival rate.

This video covers the design, the Tulsa context, the surviving airframes, and why the Executive deserves to be seen as the first modern executive aircraft.
