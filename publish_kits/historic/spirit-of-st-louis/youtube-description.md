Lindbergh wasn't the first to cross the Atlantic. He wasn't even the second. He was the 19th - but he was the first to do it solo and non-stop. And the aircraft he did it in was effectively a fuel tank with a man bolted on the back.

The Spirit of St. Louis had no forward window. Lindbergh used a periscope and side windows. The fuselage was so full of fuel he had to taxi by S-turning. Ryan Airlines built it in 60 days for $10,580 against Wright Aeronautical's brand-new Whirlwind engine - the same engine that powered six rival attempts that crashed or vanished. Then the world lost its mind for him in a way no pilot before or since has experienced.
