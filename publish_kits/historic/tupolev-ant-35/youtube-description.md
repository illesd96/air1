First flown August 20, 1936 and unveiled at the Paris Air Show that November, the Tupolev ANT-35 was Andrei Tupolev's twin-engined, all-metal, low-wing monoplane intended to modernize Aeroflot. Powered by two Gnome-Rhone 14K (or later M-85) radials, it cruised at roughly 376 km/h, faster than the Douglas DC-3, and entered service in 1937 as the PS-35. Then production stalled. Only 11 were built, in part because Aeroflot pivoted to the licensed Lisunov Li-2 (DC-3) and in part because Tupolev himself was arrested in 1937 during the Stalinist purges. In this video we examine the design's clean stressed-skin aerodynamics, the corrugated-skin legacy it left behind, the political fate of its designer in the sharashka prison bureaus, and why the Soviet Union ultimately preferred a licensed American design. We also clarify the frequent confusion between the ANT-35 and the unrelated ANT-25 record aircraft.
