The Avro Type E — later designated Type 500 and Type 502 — was the aircraft that Alliott Verdon Roe regarded as his firm's first truly successful design. First flown on 3 March 1912 at Brooklands, it was a tractor biplane (engine at the front, pulling), at a time when many British designers still preferred pusher configurations.

The Type E was important for two reasons. First, it was the immediate ancestor of the Avro 504 — which would become the most-produced British training aircraft of the First World War, with around 8,000 built. Second, it helped establish the tractor biplane layout as the dominant configuration for military aircraft, a lineage that would run unbroken through the 504, the Sopwith Camel, and ultimately the entire interwar trainer tradition.

This video covers Alliott Verdon Roe, the Brooklands aviation scene of 1912, the Type E design, and how the direct lineage from Type E to Avro 504 actually worked.
