The ANBO IV, sometimes designated ANBO 41, was a reconnaissance aircraft designed by Lithuanian engineer Antanas Gustaitis and produced in small numbers for the Lithuanian Air Force in the 1930s. By the standards of contemporary European reconnaissance designs, the ANBO IV was structurally modern, surprisingly fast, and offered an excellent climb rate. It was the product of a domestic aviation industry that was small, ambitious, and quietly competent. First flown on July 14th 1932, the ANBO IV equipped Lithuanian squadrons until the country lost its independence in 1940. The Soviet Union absorbed the Baltic states, and most of the remaining ANBO airframes were destroyed during the Second World War. This video covers Gustaitis as an aircraft designer, the technical choices that put the ANBO IV ahead of many larger countries' equivalent reconnaissance aircraft, the broader Baltic interwar aviation industry, and what happened to Lithuanian airframes after 1940. The ANBO story is a useful reminder that small countries built genuinely capable aircraft and that political collapse, not engineering failure, ended their stories.
