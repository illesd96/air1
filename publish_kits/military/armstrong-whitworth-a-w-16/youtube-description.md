The Armstrong Whitworth A.W.16 was a single-engine biplane fighter designed by the British aircraft manufacturer Armstrong Whitworth in the early 1930s. It was a clean, conventionally arranged fighter intended to win an RAF requirement that ultimately went to the Bristol Bulldog. The A.W.16 was not a bad aircraft, but it landed in a market dominated by Bristol and Hawker, and the RAF had little appetite for a third supplier. A small number found their way to China during the turbulent years of the Sino-Japanese war, where their fates dissolved into the broader chaos of the conflict. This video covers the A.W.16's design, how it stacked up against the Bulldog and other contemporaries, the political and industrial reasons Armstrong Whitworth kept losing fighter contracts in this period, and the obscure Chinese service that gave the type its only combat exposure. The A.W.16 is a textbook example of a competent design beaten by timing and industrial politics, and its story sits in the cracks between the famous interwar biplanes everyone covers.
