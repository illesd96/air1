The Bellanca Aircruiser and its earlier sibling the Airbus were single-engine high-wing utility aircraft built by Bellanca Aircraft Corporation in New Castle, Delaware during the early 1930s. They were designed as workhorses, intended for passenger or cargo work and available on wheels, floats, or skis. Powered by either a Wright Cyclone or a Pratt and Whitney Hornet, the Aircruiser could haul roughly a ton of payload, an impressive number for a single-engine machine of the era. The US military picked up a small number for utility duties, and the design found a long second life with Canadian bush operators who needed something that could carry freight into ice-bound lakes and rough strips. This video covers the Aircruiser's design lineage, the engineering choices that gave it such a high payload fraction, its limited military service, and the Canadian bush operations that kept the type flying long after its civilian heyday ended. Bellanca's later commercial success was modest, but the Aircruiser is a quiet reminder of just how capable a well-designed single could be before twin-engine cargo aircraft took over.
