The Bleriot SPAD S.81 is the aircraft that won an argument about how fast you should change. In 1923 the French Air Force needed a new fighter, and it ran a flyoff between two candidates. The Dewoitine D.1 was a parasol monoplane with a clean cantilever wing, a brave design from a brave designer. The Bleriot SPAD S.81, designed by Andre Herbemont, was a single-bay biplane of conventional configuration with I-shaped interplane struts and lacking Herbemont's signature stagger wing arrangement. The French Air Force ordered eighty S.81s and rejected the D.1, citing the Dewoitine's more radical design as a reason. In this video we cover the design choices, the comparative evaluation, the operational service with French fighter escadrilles in the mid 1920s, and the lessons about institutional risk tolerance that the S.81 procurement teaches. The Dewoitine D.1 found export success and Herbemont's later designs vindicated his approach, but in 1923 caution won, and the Bleriot SPAD S.81 is what that caution looked like.
