The Boulton Paul Balliol and its Royal Navy variant Sea Balliol were British advanced trainer aircraft from the late 1940s and 1950s. On May 17, 1948, the Balliol prototype, fitted with an Armstrong Siddeley Mamba turboprop, became the world's first single-engined turboprop aircraft to fly — a milestone almost completely buried in mainstream aviation history.

In this video we trace why the RAF backed a turboprop trainer in the first place, the political and industrial reasons production switched to a Rolls-Royce Merlin piston-engine version, the Balliol's brief service life with both RAF and Fleet Air Arm units, and how the rapid arrival of the de Havilland Vampire two-seat jet trainer quietly made the Balliol obsolete before it could fully take hold.
