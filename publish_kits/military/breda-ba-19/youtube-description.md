The Breda Ba.19 was an Italian single-seat aerobatic biplane trainer developed in 1928 by Societa Italiana Ernesto Breda, and it became one of the defining aerobatic platforms of interwar Europe. With a powerful Alfa Romeo Lynx radial engine in a clean cowling, short fuselage, and wings designed to handle sustained negative g loads, the Ba.19 was specifically built to teach advanced pilots the discipline of inverted flight. The Regia Aeronautica adopted it as its standard advanced training aircraft, and a number were also exported. Italian display pilots flew Ba.19s at air shows across Europe in the 1930s, and the type contributed to the strong tradition of Italian formation aerobatics that survives to this day. In this video we cover the design genesis, the engine and structural features that made it suitable for inverted manoeuvring, the training role, the display heritage, and the lineage that connects the Ba.19 to later Italian aerobatic aircraft. A small aircraft with an outsized influence on European pilot training.
