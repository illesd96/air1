The Bréguet 270 is the kind of aircraft that gets written out of summaries because it does not fit a clean story. Designed in 1928 by the French manufacturer Breguet under Marcel Vuillerme, the 270 was a sesquiplane reconnaissance and light bombing platform built around a metal structure when most rivals were still using wood. It replaced the Bréguet 19 in French observation squadrons and served across colonial outposts in North Africa and Indochina through the 1930s. By the time the Battle of France arrived in 1940 the 270 was already obsolete, but its airframe philosophy, all-metal construction with a smaller lower wing for visibility, fed directly into later Breguet designs. This video covers the design choices, the operational record across colonies, why the French chose a sesquiplane layout when others were moving to cantilever monoplanes, and what happened to the surviving airframes when the Luftwaffe arrived. No clickbait, no glory shots, just the honest story of an aircraft that did its job in a decade nobody talks about.
