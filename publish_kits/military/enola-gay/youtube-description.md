The Enola Gay is a Boeing B-29 Superfortress named by Colonel Paul Tibbets after his mother, Enola Gay Tibbets. On August 6th 1945, in the final weeks of the Second World War, it carried out the mission that destroyed much of Hiroshima and helped end the war in the Pacific. This video is not about glorifying that event. It is about the aircraft itself: how the B-29 program produced a machine capable of that mission, the modifications applied to the small group of Silverplate B-29s, the long postwar life of the airframe including its near-scrapping, the restoration work undertaken by the National Air and Space Museum, and the public debate that erupted when the aircraft went on display in the 1990s. The Enola Gay now sits at the Steven F. Udvar-Hazy Center in Virginia, where visitors confront a machine whose history forces honest reckoning rather than easy celebration. We will walk through the aircraft's design, mission profile, postwar handling, and museum display, with sources for further reading.
