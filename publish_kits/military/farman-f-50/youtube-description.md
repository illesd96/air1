The Farman F.50 entered service in the closing months of World War I as France's answer to a problem the British had already solved with the Handley Page O/400. The Voisin pusher biplanes that had carried the French night bombing campaign were obsolete, dangerous, and slow, and the Farman brothers, Henri and Maurice, drew the F.50 as a clean-sheet replacement. Twin Lorraine-Dietrich engines in tractor configuration, a deeper fuselage with a proper bomb bay, and accommodation for a crew of three. The first F.50 flew in 1918, and a small number reached frontline escadrilles before the Armistice. The type also saw limited postwar service in conversion to civilian transport roles. In this video we cover the design genesis, the engine and structural choices, the operational deployments in the final weeks of the war, the conversion to civilian use in the early 1920s, and the lessons that fed into the Farman F.60 Goliath. A textbook example of an aircraft whose timing kept it out of the famous stories.
