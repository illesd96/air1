The Felixstowe Porte Baby was a British reconnaissance flying boat of the First World War, designed by John Cyril Porte at the Seaplane Experimental Station at Felixstowe and first flown in 1915. It was a large three-engined biplane with a hull built for North Sea conditions, used by the Royal Naval Air Service for long-range patrol work, anti-submarine patrol, and zeppelin interception attempts. The nickname Baby was a holdover from the earlier Curtiss H-4 Small America that informed Porte's hull design work. In practice, the Porte Baby was one of the larger aircraft of the war, and it played a key role in proving the design principles that later produced the much more famous Felixstowe F.2 and F.3 patrol flying boats.
