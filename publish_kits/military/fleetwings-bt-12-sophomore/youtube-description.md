The Fleetwings BT-12 Sophomore was an all-metal basic trainer ordered by the United States Army Air Forces in the early 1940s. What set it apart was the airframe: Fleetwings had spent years experimenting with stainless steel construction, and the BT-12 used that expertise to produce a structure that was unusually strong, corrosion resistant, and almost completely unique among American trainers of the era. Only 24 production examples were built before the entire contract was cancelled. The reason was prosaic and brutal. The USAAF already had the Vultee BT-13 in massive production, training pipelines were saturated, and there was simply no slot for another basic trainer no matter how cleverly engineered. This video covers the Fleetwings company's obsession with stainless steel, the design choices that made the Sophomore interesting, why the cancellation came so quickly, and what happened to the handful of airframes that were actually delivered. The BT-12 is the kind of aircraft that pops up in lists of obscure WWII trainers and nowhere else, and its story is a useful reminder that wartime procurement rewarded scale, not novelty.
