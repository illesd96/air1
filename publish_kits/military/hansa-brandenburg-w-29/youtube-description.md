The Hansa-Brandenburg W.29 is one of the most influential aircraft of the final year of World War I and one of the least known in mainstream histories. Designed by Ernst Heinkel at Hansa und Brandenburgische Flugzeugwerke, the W.29 was a low-wing cantilever monoplane on twin main floats, powered by a 150 horsepower Benz Bz.III engine, with a single forward firing machine gun and an observer-operated gun in the rear cockpit. It entered service with the Imperial German Navy's Naval Air Service from bases on the North Sea coast in spring 1918. In a series of running engagements with British Felixstowe flying boats and Curtiss H.12s, the W.29 demonstrated that a fast monoplane floatplane could outperform any biplane seaplane in service. In this video we cover the design philosophy, the lineage from the W.12 biplane, the operational record at Zeebrugge and List, the postwar service with Finland, Latvia, and Denmark, and the influence the W.29 had on Heinkel's later monoplane floatplane designs.
