The Hiro H4H was the kind of aircraft only an in-house arsenal could produce. Hiro Naval Arsenal, one of the Imperial Japanese Navy's own aircraft factories, drew the H4H as a monoplane flying boat in 1931 for bombing and reconnaissance work. It carried a parasol wing on cabane struts above a deep hull, three Hispano-Suiza inline engines, and a crew of five or six. Production was modest, around forty seven aircraft built, and operational use focused on coastal patrol and training duties through the early to mid 1930s. In this video we cover the design context within the Hiro arsenal's broader output, the influence of British Supermarine Southampton designs on the H4H layout, the engine selection and the structural arrangement, the operational squadrons, and the transition out of service as the Kawanishi H6K Mavis began to enter service in larger numbers. A patient look at an aircraft that very few documentaries have covered seriously.
