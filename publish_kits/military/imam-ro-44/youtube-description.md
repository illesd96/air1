The IMAM Ro.44 is the kind of honest failure that teaches more than a success. In 1936 IMAM, Industrie Meccaniche e Aeronautiche Meridionali, attempted to create a single-seat fighter version of its successful Ro.43 reconnaissance seaplane. Armed with two 12.7 millimetre Breda SAFAT machine guns in the nose, the Ro.44 had the rear fuselage redesigned to remove the observer position and changes were made to the tail. The result was disappointing across the board. Overall performance was no better than the Ro.43 in most respects, manoeuvrability suffered without the observer's weight at the rear, and the heavy central float continued to limit speed and climb. The Regia Aeronautica took thirty five aircraft for service aboard the Italian battleships and cruisers, but the Ro.44 was rarely used in combat. In this video we cover the design rationale, the structural changes from the Ro.43, the operational deployment aboard specific ships, the pilot evaluations, and the lessons about derivative single-seat designs that the Ro.44 taught Italian designers.
