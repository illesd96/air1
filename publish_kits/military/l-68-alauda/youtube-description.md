The Albatros L 68 Alauda was a two-seat German trainer aircraft of the mid 1920s, built by a company best known for its First World War fighters. It was a conventional single-engine biplane with tandem open cockpits for the student and instructor, designed during a period when the Treaty of Versailles still nominally restricted German military aviation. In practice, civilian flight clubs, contracted training operations in Russia, and a thicket of nominally civil aircraft kept German aviation expertise alive throughout the Weimar era. The L 68 was one small piece of that mosaic. This video covers Albatros's transition from wartime fighter manufacturer to interwar trainer builder, the political environment that shaped what German manufacturers could and could not produce, the L 68's design and instructional role, and how trainers like the Alauda fit into the larger story of how Germany quietly maintained the pilots and engineers who would later staff the Luftwaffe.
