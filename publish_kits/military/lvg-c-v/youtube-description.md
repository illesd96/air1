The LVG C.V was a German two-seat reconnaissance biplane, mass-produced by Luft-Verkehrs-Gesellschaft and used across every front by the Imperial German Air Service from 1917 onward. It is the kind of aircraft that almost never makes the magazine covers — and almost always shapes how a war actually goes.

In this video we look at why two-seater reconnaissance was the actual decisive air activity of WWI, how artillery cooperation drove aircraft design more than dogfighting did, the LVG company itself and its prewar lineage, the C.V's Benz Bz.IV engine, and how the type's straightforward construction made it ideal for the chaotic 1918 build-up.
