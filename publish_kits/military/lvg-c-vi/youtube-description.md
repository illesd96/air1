The LVG C.VI was a refined, slightly smaller follow-on to the C.V two-seat reconnaissance and artillery-spotting biplane, built by Luft-Verkehrs-Gesellschaft for the Imperial German Air Service in the last year of the First World War. Captured examples ended up in Allied hands, and at least one surviving airframe — at the Shuttleworth Collection in England — is one of the only operable Imperial German aircraft left on the planet.

In this video we look at why the German air service iterated on the LVG family rather than replacing it outright, what the C.VI improved over the C.V, the staggering postwar story of the captured airframes (and the long Shuttleworth restoration), and why this unglamorous two-seater is now arguably more historically important than half the fighters that get all the books.
