The Macchi M.7 is the kind of aircraft that argues against neat categories. Designed by Alessandro Tonini at Macchi in Varese and first flown in 1918, it was a single-seat flying boat fighter intended for the Italian Navy. A modified racing version, the M.7bis, won the Schneider Trophy at Venice in 1921. The production M.7 then served in Italian naval fighter squadrons from 1923 to 1930, with examples also exported to Argentina, Brazil, and Sweden. In this video we walk through the design lineage from the wartime M.5, the structural changes that made the M.7 a proper fighter rather than a converted scout, the Schneider Trophy campaign of 1921 in detail, and the operational deployments through the late 1920s. We also discuss why the era of the flying boat fighter ended so abruptly with the rise of carrier aviation and dedicated floatplane interceptors. A rare example of a racer that also did its day job, the M.7 deserves more attention than it usually gets in Schneider Trophy retrospectives.
