The Nakajima E2N is the kind of bridge aircraft that most documentaries skip over but every serious aviation historian respects. Designed in the late 1920s as a reconnaissance floatplane for the Imperial Japanese Navy, it was a two-seat sesquiplane on twin main floats, powered by a Mitsubishi-built Hispano-Suiza engine. Around eighty examples were built, most by Nakajima with a smaller batch by Aichi, and the type served aboard Japanese cruisers and battleships through the early 1930s. Operationally it was unremarkable, but its real contribution was institutional. The E2N taught the Imperial Navy how to handle catapult-launched reconnaissance from capital ships, how to recover floatplanes alongside in heavy seas, and how to integrate aerial reconnaissance into fleet operations. In this video we cover the design influence of earlier Hansa-Brandenburg types, the structural details of the float and wing arrangement, the operational deployments aboard specific ships, and the lessons that fed directly into the Nakajima E4N and Kawanishi E7K successors.
