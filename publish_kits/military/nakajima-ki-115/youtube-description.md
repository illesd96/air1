The Nakajima Ki-115 Tsurugi was designed in 1945 for a single mission. Japan, by then under sustained Allied bombing and facing critical shortages of materials, fuel, and experienced pilots, asked Nakajima to design an aircraft that could be built by semi-skilled labor using non-strategic materials. The result was a low-wing monoplane with a steel tube fuselage, wooden tail surfaces, fixed jettisonable landing gear, and a single 800 horsepower Nakajima Ha-35 radial engine. Roughly 105 were built before the war ended, and none flew operationally. This video treats the Ki-115 with the seriousness the subject demands. We examine the engineering decisions, the production planning, the wider context of Japan's industrial collapse in 1945, and the postwar evaluations conducted by US and British technical missions. We also discuss the human cost behind these design choices and the surviving examples in museum collections today. The Ki-115 is not an aircraft to celebrate. It is an aircraft to understand, because understanding it explains a great deal about how wars end.
