The Nord CT.41 Narwhal is the answer to a 1950s problem that air forces around the world were grappling with. As supersonic bombers became operational, the question of how to train interceptor pilots and develop air defense doctrine against them became urgent. Building real supersonic targets was expensive and dangerous. Nord Aviation, the French state aerospace company that also built the SS.10 anti-tank missile and the Noratlas transport, took up the challenge. The CT.41 was a delta winged turbojet-powered target drone capable of high subsonic and brief supersonic flight, launched from the ground and recovered by parachute. It carried instrumentation packages that allowed ground controllers to evaluate interception attempts in detail. In this video we cover the design genesis, the engine and aerodynamic choices, the recovery system, the operational use by the Armee de l'Air and other customers in the 1960s, and what the CT.41 tells us about how Cold War air forces solved the training problem. A small aircraft with a big role in interceptor doctrine.
