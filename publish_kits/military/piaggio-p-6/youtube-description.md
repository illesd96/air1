The Piaggio P.6 was an Italian reconnaissance floatplane designed in the late 1920s for the Regia Marina, intended for catapult launch from battleships and cruisers. First flown in 1927, it was a compact single-engine biplane on twin floats, sized to fit the constrained launch envelope of Italian capital ship catapults. Only a small number were built before the Italian Navy moved on to other designs, and the P.6 remains one of the quieter chapters in Piaggio's long history. This video covers Italian interwar naval aviation doctrine, the specific role of catapult floatplanes in fleet scouting before radar made the concept obsolete, the P.6's design and limitations, and how Piaggio's company trajectory through the 1920s and 1930s shaped what aircraft it could and could not deliver. The catapult floatplane was a global phenomenon between the wars, and Italy's contributions to it are usually skipped over in favor of American, British, and Japanese types.
