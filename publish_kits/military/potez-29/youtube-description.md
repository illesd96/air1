The Potez 29 is the aircraft you build when you stop pretending civilian and military aviation are separate worlds. Henry Potez drew it as a five-seat airliner in 1927, scaling up his successful Potez 25, and it flew with Air Union and other early carriers. But the real story is what happened next. The French Air Force bought the 29 by the hundreds, converting it into air ambulances that evacuated wounded across Morocco, into navigator and gunnery trainers, into colonial liaison aircraft in Indochina, and into long-distance record platforms. One Potez 29 flew Paris to Saint Louis du Senegal in 1928, helping to map out future Aeropostale routes. This video covers the variants in detail, the operators across French colonies and beyond, the cabin layouts for medical and transport use, and why a humble cabin biplane outlasted many of its purpose-built military contemporaries.
