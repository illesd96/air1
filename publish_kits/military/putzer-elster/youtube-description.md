The Putzer Elster is a small aircraft with a large symbolic weight. After 1945, Germany was prohibited from designing and producing aircraft under the terms of the postwar occupation, a restriction that was partially lifted only in 1955 with West Germany's accession to NATO. Alfons Putzer KG of Bonn was one of the small companies that took advantage of the new freedom, drawing the Elster as a single-engine light aircraft for recreational flying and military liaison work. The prototype first flew on 10 January 1959, and the Bundesluftwaffe and Marineflieger took delivery of small batches for sport and liaison duties. In this video we cover the regulatory context that shaped postwar German aviation, the design choices Putzer made for a low-cost trainer, the military operators and their unusual use of an essentially recreational airframe, the production history, and the small but devoted survivor community still flying Elsters in Germany today. A modest aircraft that represents a much larger story about national aviation industries.
