The Saro Cloud was a small British passenger amphibian flying boat designed and built by Saunders-Roe on the Isle of Wight. It started as the A.19 for civil customers, where it found a tiny market, but its real significance came when the RAF adopted a militarized version as the A.29 to train pilots and navigators on the peculiar discipline of flying boat operations. Before a crew climbed into a Sunderland or a Lerwick, somebody had to teach them how to land on choppy water, how to taxi a hull, and how to navigate over open ocean. The Cloud did that job quietly through the 1930s and into the early war years. This video covers the Cloud's civilian origins, the RAF's training pipeline for flying boats, why dedicated trainers were essential before crews moved up to the big patrol types, and what happened to the surviving airframes. The Cloud is exactly the kind of supporting aircraft that gets squeezed out of every general history of Coastal Command, which is why its story matters.
