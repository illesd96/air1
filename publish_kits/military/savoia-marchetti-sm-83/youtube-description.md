The Savoia-Marchetti SM.83 was an Italian three-engine civil airliner of the late 1930s, derived directly from the SM.79 Sparviero medium bomber. It kept the trimotor layout, the humpbacked profile, and most of the SM.79's speed, but stripped out the gun positions and bomb bay to make room for passenger seats. First flown in November 1937, the SM.83 served on Italian and South American routes, where its speed and range made it genuinely competitive against the all-metal Douglas and Lockheed twins flooding the market. The total production run was tiny, the war pulled most surviving examples into military transport service, and the type effectively disappeared from civil aviation by the early 1940s. This video covers how the SM.79 was adapted into the SM.83, the route networks it actually flew, why Italy struggled to break into the export airliner market, and what happened to the surviving airframes during the war. The SM.83 is a fascinating case of military lineage masquerading as civilian travel, and it deserves a much closer look than the usual one-paragraph treatment.
