The SIAI S.8 is one of those quiet machines that built an air force without ever winning a battle. Italian designers at Societa Idrovolanti Alta Italia drew this two-seat reconnaissance flying boat in 1917, putting a pusher engine above a varnished wooden hull and sending it out over the Adriatic to spot submarines and ships. It never appeared in the recruiting posters. Italian naval crews learned the unforgiving discipline of water landings in S.8s before they were trusted with anything faster. The wing structure was a direct evolution of the FBA Type H, and the lessons SIAI took from the S.8 fed directly into the more famous S.13 and S.16. In this video we walk through the construction, the role it filled in coastal patrol squadrons, what surviving photographs tell us about the cockpit layout, and why a humble trainer-reconnaissance machine deserves a place in the story of Italian aviation.
