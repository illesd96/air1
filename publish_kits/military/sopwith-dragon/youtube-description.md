The Sopwith Dragon was a British single-seat fighter biplane developed from the Sopwith Snipe and powered by the ABC Dragonfly radial engine. It entered limited Royal Air Force service shortly after the First World War and was withdrawn quickly, a casualty of one of the most notorious engine programmes in British aviation history.

In this video we look at the late-war British faith in the ABC Dragonfly — a radial engine that politicians and procurement officials believed would transform RAF combat aviation — and the painful realisation that the engine overheated, vibrated, and could not be made reliable. We trace how the Dragon airframe inherited that problem, why Sopwith's design team could not save it, and how the experience shaped postwar British engine policy and the rise of Bristol and Armstrong Siddeley radial designs.
