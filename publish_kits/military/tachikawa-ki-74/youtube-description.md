The Tachikawa Ki-74 was a Japanese experimental long-range, high-altitude reconnaissance bomber developed during the Second World War for the Imperial Japanese Army Air Service. It was a twin-engine mid-wing monoplane with a pressurized cabin, intended to fly extreme-range reconnaissance missions and, in some configurations, to deliver a small bomb load over very long distances. There were periodic discussions about whether the Ki-74 could reach the continental United States; in practice the program ran into engine reliability problems, repeated delays, and the broader collapse of the Japanese war economy. Only a small number were completed, none saw combat, and the surviving airframes were captured at the end of the war. This video covers the Ki-74's design, the engineering choices required to achieve long range and high-altitude flight in 1944 Japan, the proposed mission profiles including the trans-Pacific concept, why the program never delivered operational aircraft, and what happened to the prototypes after the war.
