# a-32-bussard
## Hooks
- The Focke-Wulf A 32 Bussard was rushed into production in 1930 only because Messerschmitt failed to deliver airliners on time to the Norwegian operator NOBA — a contract failure that gave Focke-Wulf its first airliner.
- Only two A 32s were ever built — both for NOBA — and they ended up in Deutsche Luft Hansa's fleet when NOBA folded, becoming Focke-Wulf's first commercial aircraft in airline service.
- The A 32 was essentially a Focke-Wulf A 20 with a fatter fuselage and an engine more than twice as powerful — an early example of the engineering trick of upsizing a working prototype to meet a customer's demand.
- Focke-Wulf's chief designer Heinrich Focke would later become world-famous for the Fw 200 Condor and the first practical helicopter — the Bussard was the project that proved his airliner credentials.
- A 32 production was specifically a contract-of-opportunity job — Norwegian operator NOBA needed planes immediately, and the Bussard was a one-off lucky break that put Focke-Wulf on the German aviation map.

## Seed paragraph

In 1930, a Norwegian airline called NOBA was desperate. Messerschmitt had failed to deliver the airliners they'd ordered. A small German company called Focke-Wulf — better known later for fighters — said they could deliver in months by stretching an existing design. Only two Bussards were ever built, but the contract launched Focke-Wulf as a commercial aircraft maker. This is the story of how a missed deadline created an aviation dynasty.
