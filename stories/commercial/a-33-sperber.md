# a-33-sperber
## Hooks
- Only three Focke-Wulf A 33 Sperbers were ever built — each one custom-ordered by a different German air-taxi operator in the early 1930s, making it one of the rarest commercial designs of its era.
- The Sperber was essentially a scaled-down sibling of the A 32 Bussard — a high-wing cantilever monoplane that Focke-Wulf built when air-taxi demand outpaced full-size airliner orders.
- One of the three A 33s briefly served with Deutsche Luft Hansa in 1937 — meaning the type entered Germany's national airline as an aging, second-hand bushplane rather than a flagship purchase.
- The A 33's air-taxi role represented an entire lost category of 1930s aviation — short, low-volume premium routes between German cities that vanished when Lufthansa's standardized fleet swallowed everything.
- The Sperber name (German for 'sparrowhawk') was part of Focke-Wulf's bird-themed naming tradition — Bussard (buzzard), Möwe (gull), Stieglitz (goldfinch), Stösser (merlin) — that lasted until WWII forced military designations.

## Seed paragraph

In the early 1930s, before Lufthansa swallowed every German operator, there was a brief golden age of small private air-taxi companies. The Focke-Wulf A 33 Sperber was built for them — only three were ever made, each for a different operator. It is the smallest, rarest, most obscure airliner in Focke-Wulf's history. And yet one of them ended its life in Lufthansa colors — the survivor of an entire vanished aviation ecosystem.
