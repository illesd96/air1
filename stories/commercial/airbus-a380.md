# airbus-a380
## Hooks
- Airbus bet billions that hub-and-spoke would dominate aviation, but the moment the 777 and 787 made point-to-point twinjets profitable on long routes, the A380's business case evaporated before it even hit full production rate.
- Early A380s were delivered to Singapore Airlines roughly two years late because two Airbus design teams in Germany and France used incompatible versions of CATIA software and the wiring harnesses literally did not fit when fuselages met in Toulouse.
- Emirates was effectively the only airline that loved the jet — its order for 142 frames kept the program alive for years, and when Tim Clark finally walked away in 2019, Airbus announced production would end within hours.
- The first prototype's wing snapped at 1.45 times limit load during static testing in 2006, just short of the certification target, forcing engineers to add reinforcement that ate into payload margin.
- Many A380s went straight from delivery to the scrapyard in Tarbes after barely a decade in service, with engines and parts worth more than the airframe — a humiliating end for the most expensive airliner ever built.
- The double-deck shape forced a complete rebuild of airport infrastructure worldwide: new jet bridges, reinforced taxiways, and the famous Group F gate code — costs that some airports never recouped.

## Seed paragraph

Airbus spent 25 billion dollars building the largest passenger plane in history — and a software incompatibility between its French and German factories meant the wiring didn't reach. By the time they fixed it, Boeing's twin-engine 777 had already won the war. This is the story of how the king of the skies became the most expensive aluminum scrap heap in aviation.
