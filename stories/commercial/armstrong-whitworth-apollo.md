# armstrong-whitworth-apollo
## Hooks
- The Armstrong Whitworth Apollo was a 1949 British four-engine turboprop airliner that lost the race against the Vickers Viscount because its Armstrong Siddeley Mamba engines suffered chronic vibration and reliability problems.
- Only two Apollos were ever built — both ended up at the Royal Aircraft Establishment as research test-beds, never carrying a single fare-paying passenger.
- The Apollo and Viscount were direct competitors developed under the same Ministry of Supply specification — and the Apollo's defeat handed Vickers a near-monopoly on the world's first successful turboprop airliner.
- Armstrong Whitworth's failure with the Apollo effectively ended the Coventry firm's career as an independent airliner manufacturer — the company was absorbed into Hawker Siddeley in 1961.
- The Apollo had to be redesigned mid-development to accommodate the Mamba engines' shifting performance numbers — Armstrong Whitworth engineers were chasing a moving target Armstrong Siddeley couldn't deliver.

## Seed paragraph

In 1949 Britain, two four-engine turboprop airliners flew off against each other for the future of post-war commercial flight. The Vickers Viscount became the most successful British airliner ever built. The Armstrong Whitworth Apollo? Two of them ever rolled out, neither ever sold to an airline. Both ended their lives as test-beds at the Royal Aircraft Establishment — quiet ghosts of a future that British aviation never got to fly.
