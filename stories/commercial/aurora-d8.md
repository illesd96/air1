# aurora-d8
## Hooks
- The Aurora D8 — nicknamed the 'double bubble' — has two parallel fuselages fused side-by-side, like two narrow-bodies welded together, and three engines mounted on top of the rear fuselage to ingest the boundary-layer airflow.
- MIT, Aurora Flight Sciences, and Pratt & Whitney designed the D8 under NASA's $2.9 million N+3 program — the goal was a 70% fuel-burn reduction versus a 737 by 2035, the most ambitious efficiency target in airliner history.
- Boundary-layer ingestion — sucking the slow air off the top of the fuselage into the engines — has never been done in a commercial airliner because the unsteady airflow chews up fan blades, a problem the D8 team is still trying to solve.
- The D8's lift comes partly from the wide, flat fuselage itself — a 'lifting body' concept that erases the line between wing and cabin and could let regional planes use shorter runways.
- Despite a decade of NASA funding, no full-scale D8 has been built — it remains a wind-tunnel and simulator program with no production timetable, a reminder that radical airliner concepts almost never reach commercial service.

## Seed paragraph

What if you welded two narrow-body airliners side-by-side, put the engines on top, and let the fuselage itself create lift? That's the Aurora D8 — NASA's pet bet on the airliner of 2035, designed by MIT and Pratt & Whitney to burn 70 percent less fuel than a Boeing 737. It would be the strangest airliner ever built. The catch: no one has built one yet, and it's been more than fifteen years.
