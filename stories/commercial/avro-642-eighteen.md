# avro-642-eighteen
## Hooks
- The Avro 642 Eighteen — named for its 18 passenger seats — was built in only two examples, one with two engines and one with four, an unusual hedging of bets by Avro that revealed deep uncertainty about the airliner market in 1934.
- The original 642 had a glazed circular nose like a bomber — pilots disliked the visibility distortion, and Avro rebuilt the nose to a more conventional shape on the production aircraft.
- The 642 reused the wing from the Avro 618 Ten, which was itself a licensed copy of the Dutch Fokker F.VIIb — meaning the Eighteen was a third-generation derivative of a 1920s Fokker design.
- One of the two Eighteens was bought by the Viceroy of India for personal use — making the Avro 642 one of the earliest 'head of state' VIP airliners in history.
- Only producing two aircraft made the 642 commercially insignificant, but its engineering experiments — twin vs. four-engine configurations on the same airframe — informed Avro's later York and Tudor designs.

## Seed paragraph

Only two Avro Eighteens were ever built — one with two engines, one with four — and Avro genuinely couldn't decide which version was better. The original glazed bomber-nose cockpit had to be torn off and rebuilt because pilots couldn't see properly through it. One example ended up as the personal aircraft of the Viceroy of India. It's a forgotten little airliner from a Britain that was still figuring out what an airliner should look like.
