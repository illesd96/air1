# avro-652
## Hooks
- The Avro 652 was a 1934 twin-engine airliner that only sold two units to Imperial Airways — but its airframe became the basis for the Avro Anson, of which 11,000 were built as RAF maritime patrol and trainer aircraft.
- The 652 was Britain's first low-wing cantilever monoplane airliner with retractable landing gear — combining three modern features that British aviation had previously been slow to adopt.
- Avro derived the Anson from the 652 in just four months in 1935 to win an Air Ministry contract — making the airliner one of the most cost-effective platforms-to-military-variants in aviation history.
- Imperial Airways used the two 652s for the Croydon-to-Brindisi-to-Cairo route, replacing slower biplanes — a brief but elite commercial career before they were absorbed into wartime training fleets.
- The 652 marked the moment when British civil airliner design finally caught up with American Douglas DC-2 standards — though too late commercially to challenge them.

## Seed paragraph

Imperial Airways bought only two of them. The Avro 652 was a commercial failure — a small twin-engine airliner that lost the market race to American Douglas designs. But four months after Avro shipped the second 652, the Air Ministry asked for a maritime patrol plane. Avro pulled the 652 blueprints off the shelf, modified them, and produced the Anson — eleven thousand of which would patrol the seas in World War II. The airliner that lost commercial built the warplane that won the Atlantic.
