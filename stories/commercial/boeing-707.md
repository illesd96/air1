# boeing-707
## Hooks
- Tex Johnston, Boeing's chief test pilot, did a full barrel-roll in the Dash 80 prototype over Lake Washington in front of airline executives in 1955 — Bill Allen reportedly said only 'Tex, please don't do that again' but the stunt sold the airliner.
- The 707 prototype only existed because Boeing privately spent $16 million — three-quarters of the company's post-war profit — on a single tanker-airliner prototype with no orders, betting the company on jets before anyone wanted them.
- Pan Am's order in 1955 was for 20 707s AND 25 Douglas DC-8s — Juan Trippe deliberately hedged because he didn't trust Boeing yet, and the resulting price war between Douglas and Boeing nearly killed both companies.
- The 707 was so noisy that early jet airports built berms and curfews specifically because of it — the JT3 engines created the 'jet noise complaints' lawsuit category in American property law.
- KC-135 tankers, derived from the same Dash 80 prototype, are still in U.S. Air Force service in 2026 — meaning a 1954 design still refuels American fighters two generations later.
- When the prototype first flew on July 15, 1954, Boeing was so cash-strapped they painted only the top half of the fuselage to save money, calling it 'production representative finish' to hide the gamble.

## Seed paragraph

In 1955, a Boeing test pilot rolled a four-engine prototype upside down over Lake Washington in front of every airline executive in America. Boeing's CEO nearly had a heart attack. But the 707 he had just risked the entire company on would launch the Jet Age, end the era of three-day Atlantic crossings, and put a noisy yellow stripe of soot across the sky from London to New York.
