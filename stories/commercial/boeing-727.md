# boeing-727
## Hooks
- On November 24, 1971, a man calling himself Dan Cooper hijacked Northwest Orient Flight 305, collected $200,000 ransom, and parachuted out of the rear airstair into the night over Washington — never found, and the FBI closed the case in 2016 unsolved.
- Boeing modified every 727 worldwide after the Cooper hijacking with the 'Cooper vane' — a wedge-shaped aerodynamic lock that physically prevents the rear airstair from being lowered in flight.
- The 727's three engines and T-tail were deliberately chosen to operate from short, hot, and high-altitude airports like LaGuardia and Mexico City — letting it land on runways where 707s couldn't.
- The first 727 had three of the most advanced devices of the era — leading-edge slats, triple-slotted Fowler flaps, and a movable horizontal stabilizer — making it a high-lift miracle for its time but a maintenance nightmare for airlines.
- Eastern Airlines Flight 401's L-1011 crash often gets the headlines, but the 727 had its own Everglades nightmare in Eastern 855 — and a string of early stall-related accidents that taught the industry about deep-stall recovery.
- FedEx kept ageing 727s in cargo service until 2013 — the trijet's ability to use small airports made it the perfect overnight freighter until newer twins finally beat its fuel economy.

## Seed paragraph

On the night before Thanksgiving 1971, a man in a black tie boarded a Boeing 727 in Portland, told a stewardess he had a bomb, took $200,000 in cash, and stepped out of the rear stairway into a storm over the Pacific Northwest. He has never been found. The hijack changed every 727 in the world — and gave the trijet a permanent place in American folklore.
