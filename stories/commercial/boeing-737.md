# boeing-737
## Hooks
- The MCAS system that crashed Lion Air 610 and Ethiopian 302 existed because the 737 MAX's huge LEAP engines had to be mounted forward and high on the wing — pitch-up tendency that didn't exist in a 1960s-era airframe scaled past its limits.
- The original 737-100 had the engines slung directly under and tight against the wings because the landing gear was so short — a feature that began with the 707 fuselage diameter Boeing reused to save money.
- Southwest Airlines bet everything on the 737 — an all-737 fleet that allowed it to invent the low-cost-carrier playbook, training every pilot, mechanic, and gate agent on one airframe and cutting turnaround time to 10 minutes.
- When the 737 MAX was grounded worldwide on March 13, 2019, it became the longest fleet grounding in commercial aviation history at 20 months, and forced Boeing CEO Dennis Muilenburg's firing on Christmas Eve 2019.
- The 737 outsold the Airbus A320 for decades partly because Boeing convinced regulators the MAX needed no new pilot type rating — the same shortcut that killed 346 people.
- Aloha Airlines Flight 243 in 1988 had 18 feet of fuselage roof peel off in cruise over Hawaii — only one person died, and the disaster created the FAA's Aging Aircraft program that still governs every commercial jet today.

## Seed paragraph

In 1967, Boeing built a stubby little jet for short hops and slapped the engines tight against the wings to save weight. Fifty years later, those same low-slung wings forced engineers to fake the physics of a fly-by-wire system on a non-fly-by-wire plane — and 346 people died because nobody told the pilots it existed. This is how the world's best-selling jetliner became the world's most controversial.
