# boeing-747
## Hooks
- The 747's iconic hump exists because Boeing's Joe Sutter expected freighter sales to dominate and put the cockpit on a second deck so the entire nose could swing up to load shipping containers — passenger lounges were an afterthought to use the leftover space.
- Boeing nearly went bankrupt building the 747; the Everett factory was the largest building by volume in the world but Pan Am's huge order, signed by Juan Trippe on a handshake with Bill Allen, was the only thing that funded it.
- When Pratt & Whitney's JT9D engines first arrived, they had so much thrust they bent themselves out of shape on the wing pylons during ground runs — and the first 747s sat at Paine Field with concrete blocks hanging where engines should be.
- Joe Sutter and his team — known as 'The Incredibles' — designed the entire 747 in just 28 months, using over 50,000 engineers, mechanics, and secretaries, and they invented modern parallel-track project management to do it.
- The 747 was supposed to be obsoleted by the Boeing 2707 supersonic transport — which is exactly why Sutter designed a fuselage cross-section that could carry freight containers two-wide, ready to become a cargo plane when SSTs took over passenger travel.
- Two 747s collided on the runway at Tenerife in 1977 in fog, killing 583 people — still the deadliest aviation accident in history, and the trigger for the entire modern Crew Resource Management discipline.

## Seed paragraph

Boeing bet the entire company on a plane Juan Trippe of Pan Am sketched on a napkin. They built the world's largest factory in a forest in Washington, hired 50,000 people, and the engines didn't work. On a wet morning in 1969, the queen of the skies almost killed her maker — and then she opened the world to economy-class travel forever.
