# boeing-777
## Hooks
- United Airlines flew the 777 across the Pacific from day one of service in 1995 on 180-minute ETOPS — the first new airliner ever certified for extended twin-engine operations on its launch day, blowing apart FAA tradition.
- The GE90 engine on the 777-300ER is so wide its fan disc has a larger diameter than the entire fuselage of a Boeing 737, and a single fan blade is roughly the length of a Toyota Camry.
- MH370 disappeared off radar on March 8, 2014 with 239 people aboard and despite Inmarsat 'handshake' pings tracing it to the southern Indian Ocean, the wreck has never been found — the most mysterious airliner loss in modern history.
- The 777 was the first commercial jet designed entirely on computers using CATIA, with no full-scale physical mockup — when the first one rolled out of the Everett factory, the doors and panels fit on the first try.
- Boeing built a 'Working Together' team that put eight launch airlines (led by United's Gordon Bethune) inside the design room from day one, ending Boeing's traditional 'here is what you ordered' relationship with customers.
- Asiana 214's landing accident at SFO in 2013 — three killed when the aircraft hit the seawall short of the runway — exposed how heavily 777 crews had grown to rely on autothrottle modes most pilots only half-understood.

## Seed paragraph

On March 8, 2014, the largest twin-engine jet ever built — a Boeing 777 carrying 239 people from Kuala Lumpur to Beijing — turned left and flew away from civilization until it ran out of fuel over an ocean nobody was watching. A decade later, we still don't know where it went. But MH370 isn't the strangest thing about the 777 — the strangest thing is that the entire jet was designed by airlines, not Boeing.
