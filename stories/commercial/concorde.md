# concorde
## Hooks
- Concorde's nose actually drooped on hydraulics for takeoff and landing because the sharp delta wing forced such a high angle of attack that pilots couldn't see the runway over the pointed supersonic profile.
- On July 25, 2000, Air France Flight 4590 ran over a titanium strip that had fallen off a Continental Airlines DC-10 minutes earlier, blew a tyre, and the rubber shrapnel ruptured a fuel tank — 109 people died and Concorde never recovered commercially.
- Concorde flew so fast that the airframe stretched roughly 25 centimetres in cruise from skin friction heating, and crews ritually wedged a cap into the gap that opened between the flight engineer's panel and the bulkhead.
- British Airways quietly made Concorde profitable in the late 1980s only after market research revealed passengers had no idea what the ticket cost — so BA raised fares dramatically and demand actually rose.
- The Soviet Tu-144 first flew two months before Concorde and Boeing's 2707 SST was canceled in 1971 over noise and ozone concerns, leaving the Anglo-French jet as the lone supersonic survivor.
- Concorde's afterburners — required only on takeoff and to push through Mach 1 — guzzled fuel so fast that one transatlantic crossing used roughly the same kerosene as a 747 carrying five times as many passengers.

## Seed paragraph

A strip of metal the size of a ruler fell off a DC-10 on a Paris runway in July 2000. Four minutes later, the most beautiful airliner ever built was on fire and falling toward a hotel. But the real reason Concorde died wasn't that crash — it was a single phone call from Airbus three years later that pulled the parts supply, and the queen of speed was condemned by her own builder.
