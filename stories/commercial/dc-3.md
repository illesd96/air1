# dc-3
## Hooks
- C.R. Smith of American Airlines literally bullied Donald Douglas into stretching the DC-2 into a sleeper aircraft over a three-hour phone call that cost Smith $300 in 1934 — and that bet created the first airliner to make money carrying only passengers, no mail subsidy needed.
- Of roughly 16,000 DC-3 / C-47s built, several hundred are still airworthy in 2026 — flying cargo in the Yukon, bush ops in Africa, and skydiver lifts in Florida, a 90-year-old design that simply refuses to die.
- C-47s flew over 'The Hump' from India to China during WWII through Himalayan thunderstorms and Japanese fighters — pilots called the route the 'aluminum trail' because so many wrecks lined the mountains.
- On D-Day, around 1,000 C-47s dropped 13,000 paratroopers over Normandy on the night of June 5–6, 1944 — Eisenhower called the DC-3/C-47 one of the four weapons that won the war.
- Basler Turbo Conversions in Wisconsin will still take a 1940s DC-3 airframe, stretch it, and fit PT6 turboprop engines — selling 'new' DC-3s in 2026 to operators who need a plane that lasts another 40 years.
- The Berlin Airlift's main workhorse was the C-47 — and Gail Halvorsen's 'Operation Little Vittles' (the Candy Bomber) used the same plane to parachute chocolate to kids on the West Berlin runway approach.

## Seed paragraph

In 1934, an airline president made an angry phone call to an aircraft manufacturer and demanded a plane that could fly people for money without government subsidy. Donald Douglas told him it couldn't be done. The DC-3 went on to win World War II, fly the Berlin Airlift, and — incredibly — is still flying cargo in Canada today, 90 years later. No airliner has ever been more important. None ever will.
