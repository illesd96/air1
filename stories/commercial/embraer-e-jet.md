# embraer-e-jet
## Hooks
- Embraer was a near-bankrupt state-owned company in the 1990s that was privatized for under $200 million — by 2005, the E-Jet had made it the third-largest commercial aircraft manufacturer in the world.
- The E-Jet's 2-2 seating layout — four-abreast with no middle seat — was designed specifically because U.S. regional pilot 'scope clauses' capped regional jets at 76 seats, and Embraer engineered the E170 to maximize comfort within that legal ceiling.
- JetBlue bet its entire growth strategy on the E190 in 2005 as a complement to its A320s, but the airline eventually divested the E-Jets in the 2020s because operating two fleet types broke its cost model.
- When Boeing tried to buy Embraer's commercial division in 2019–2020 for $4.2 billion, the deal collapsed at the height of the MAX crisis — Boeing walked away citing 'breached conditions' but most analysts believe Boeing simply ran out of cash.
- The E2 generation introduced 'staggered' business class — a layout no other regional jet has — letting tiny regional airlines offer flat-bed-style seating on routes that used to be in narrow-body coach class.
- An E190 crashed in Kazakhstan in December 2024 after suspected Russian air-defense damage, and the wreckage pattern — shrapnel holes in the tail — became a major piece of evidence in the Azerbaijan Airlines investigation.

## Seed paragraph

In the 1990s, Embraer was a Brazilian government white elephant nobody would buy. Twenty years later, it was building the only regional jet on Earth designed around an American union contract clause. Then Boeing tried to swallow it, the MAX crisis hit, and the deal collapsed mid-flight. The E-Jet is the unlikely success story of the modern regional skies — and a four-abreast loophole.
