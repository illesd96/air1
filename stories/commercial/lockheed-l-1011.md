# lockheed-l-1011
## Hooks
- The TriStar's Rolls-Royce RB211 engines bankrupted Rolls-Royce in 1971 — the British government had to nationalize the company mid-program to save the L-1011, and the U.S. Congress had to pass an emergency loan guarantee to save Lockheed.
- Eastern Airlines Flight 401 crashed into the Everglades on December 29, 1972 because the entire flight crew was focused on a burned-out landing-gear bulb and nobody noticed the autopilot had been bumped into a slow descent — killing 101 and creating modern Cockpit Resource Management.
- Delta Air Lines mechanic Cecil Hollis modified the L-1011's autoland system into the first true Cat IIIb-capable airliner — the TriStar could land in zero visibility years before its rivals.
- Lockheed sold only 250 L-1011s while McDonnell Douglas sold 446 DC-10s — and the split market between two near-identical trijets killed Lockheed's civil aviation business permanently in 1983.
- Stargazer, a modified L-1011, still launches Pegasus rockets to orbit in 2026 — the only surviving TriStar in active service is a satellite launcher, not an airliner.
- The L-1011-500 was a long-range stretched-fuselage variant that pioneered active aileron control — a passive system that detected wing flexing and reduced gust loads decades before fly-by-wire.

## Seed paragraph

Two American companies built nearly identical wide-body trijets at the same time, splitting the market between them and bankrupting one. But the L-1011's real villain was British: the Rolls-Royce engines that were supposed to power it took down Rolls-Royce, took down Lockheed's commercial division, and almost took down the British government with them. It was the best plane nobody bought.
