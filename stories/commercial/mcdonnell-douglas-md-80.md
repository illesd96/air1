# mcdonnell-douglas-md-80
## Hooks
- American Airlines bought 260 MD-80s in the 1980s — its largest single-aircraft order ever at the time — making the 'Mad Dog' the backbone of American's domestic fleet for 30 years until retirement in 2019.
- The MD-80's rear-mounted Pratt & Whitney JT8D engines gave the cabin a famously quiet front section and an unbearably loud back row — frequent fliers learned to never book past row 25.
- Alaska Airlines Flight 261 crashed off Point Mugu in January 2000 when an under-greased jackscrew in the horizontal stabilizer stripped its threads — 88 died and the MD-80 family was ordered into an emergency stabilizer inspection program.
- The MD-80 was directly descended from the 1965 Douglas DC-9 — making American Airlines' last revenue flight in 2019 the end of a 54-year-old design lineage.
- When a 1989 American Airlines pilots' strike ended, the MD-80 fleet was so central that the airline ran TV ads featuring the planes' rear-mounted engines as a symbol of return to service.
- Despite Boeing absorbing McDonnell Douglas in 1997, the MD-80 production line in Long Beach kept building 'Mad Dogs' as the Boeing 717 until 2006 — the last DC-9 derivative ever made.

## Seed paragraph

On a clear January day in 2000, Alaska Airlines Flight 261 fell out of the sky upside down because a single threaded shaft — the jackscrew — had been quietly grinding itself to powder for years. The MD-80 family had soldiered on since 1965 as the cheapest twin to operate in America. The jackscrew failure ended its safety reputation. But American Airlines kept flying Mad Dogs for nineteen more years anyway.
