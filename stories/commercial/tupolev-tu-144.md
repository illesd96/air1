# tupolev-tu-144
## Hooks
- Soviet intelligence stole so much from the Concorde program through Aeroflot employees and French engineers that the British nicknamed the Tu-144 'Concordski' — and the KGB operation was code-named 'Brand'.
- On June 3, 1973 at the Paris Air Show, the Tu-144 broke apart mid-air in front of horrified crowds, killing six crew and eight people on the ground — possibly because a French Mirage chase plane forced an emergency maneuver that exceeded G-limits.
- The Tu-144 needed canard fins that popped out behind the cockpit at low speeds — Concorde never had them, and the difference reveals just how badly Soviet engineers were guessing at aerodynamic data they couldn't actually steal.
- Tu-144 passenger service lasted only 55 commercial flights between Moscow and Alma-Ata in 1977–78 — the cabin was so loud passengers had to write notes to each other to talk, and tickets cost two months of an average Soviet salary.
- NASA leased a refurbished Tu-144LL in the 1990s as a supersonic research platform — paying Tupolev to fly the very plane the West had once tried to keep secret from.
- A second Tu-144 crashed in 1978 during a delivery flight, ending all passenger service — the official Soviet explanation took nearly 20 years to surface.

## Seed paragraph

The KGB code-named it Operation Brand: steal the Concorde, build it first, humiliate the West. The Soviets succeeded — the Tu-144 flew two months before Concorde. Then it broke apart at the Paris Air Show in front of the very engineers it had been copied from. Decades later, NASA paid the Soviets to fly the plane they once tried to bury.
