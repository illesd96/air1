# 14-bis
## Hooks
- On October 23, 1906, Alberto Santos-Dumont flew the 14-bis 60 meters over a Paris park in front of a witnessing crowd, becoming the first man to make a heavier-than-air powered flight publicly recorded in Europe.
- Santos-Dumont insisted the Wright brothers' secretive 1903 flights didn't really count because they were assisted by a catapult and rail — a controversy still alive in Brazilian schoolbooks today.
- The airplane was a 'canard' biplane that flew tail-first with the elevator on a long boom out in front, looking like a giant box kite being pushed backwards.
- Santos-Dumont was a wealthy Brazilian heir who lived in Paris and built airplanes as a hobby, having previously flown his own personal dirigible around the Eiffel Tower in 1901.
- In Brazil, Santos-Dumont — not the Wrights — is considered the father of aviation, and a replica 14-bis hangs in his honor at the National Congress in Brasília.

## Seed paragraph

Paris, October 23, 1906. A dapper Brazilian millionaire in a Panama hat climbs into a strange box-kite airplane that flies tail-first. A crowd of witnesses watches as the machine bumps along the lawn at Bagatelle and lifts off, traveling 60 meters before settling back down. In Brazil they will teach for the next century that this was the first real airplane flight — not whatever the Wrights did three years earlier behind closed doors in North Carolina.
