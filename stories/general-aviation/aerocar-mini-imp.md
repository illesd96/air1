# aerocar-mini-imp
## Hooks
- The Mini-IMP is a one-seat homebuilt where the engine sits inside the fuselage behind the pilot and a long driveshaft turns a pusher propeller mounted on the tail.
- Designer Molt Taylor — better known for his roadable Aerocar flying car — drew the Mini-IMP as a downsized version of his original IMP, with the same bizarre layout.
- The IMP family also pioneered an inverted V-tail, which Taylor argued was more efficient than a conventional cruciform tail despite looking like a stunt geometry exercise.
- Taylor sold the plans through the back pages of homebuilder magazines in the 1970s, and a handful of builders actually completed and flew Mini-IMPs from suburban driveways.
- Today fewer than ten Mini-IMPs are still flying, making them a cult curiosity at fly-ins where pilots squint at the long driveshaft tunnel and ask 'wait, where's the engine?'

## Seed paragraph

Molt Taylor was the man who built America's flying car — the Aerocar — back in the 1950s. When that didn't quite catch on, he turned to drawing strange little homebuilts. The Mini-IMP looked like a fighter, except the engine was buried inside the fuselage, and a long driveshaft ran six feet down to a propeller mounted on the tail above an inverted V-tail. Only a handful were ever finished. Every single one still draws a crowd.
