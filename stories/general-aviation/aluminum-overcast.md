# aluminum-overcast
## Hooks
- Aluminum Overcast is one of fewer than a dozen B-17 Flying Fortresses still flying today, owned and flown by the Experimental Aircraft Association out of Oshkosh, Wisconsin.
- Despite wearing the colors of the 398th Bomb Group, this particular B-17G was delivered too late to see combat — it rolled off the Vega assembly line in May 1945, just as Germany surrendered.
- After the war the airplane was sold for $750 as scrap, then spent decades as an aerial mapping platform, mosquito sprayer and cargo hauler before being restored for the EAA.
- The EAA flew Aluminum Overcast around the country giving paid B-17 rides until 2021, when the airplane was retired from flying tours due to the rarity and risk of losing it.
- She now lives on permanent display at the EAA Aviation Museum in Oshkosh, where every July a million people walk through her bomb bay during AirVenture.

## Seed paragraph

She rolled out of a California assembly line in May 1945, three weeks too late to drop a single bomb on Germany. Sold for scrap, turned into a mosquito sprayer, hauled cargo, mapped jungles. Decades later the Experimental Aircraft Association picked her up, painted her in 398th Bomb Group colors, and started taking paying passengers up for $475 a head. They called her Aluminum Overcast, and she became one of the last B-17 Flying Fortresses still in the sky.
