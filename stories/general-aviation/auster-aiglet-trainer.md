# auster-aiglet-trainer
## Hooks
- The Aiglet Trainer was Auster's 1950s answer to the postwar British civilian training market, a four-seat high-wing tube-and-fabric airplane built at Rearsby in Leicestershire.
- Despite being marketed as a trainer, the Aiglet Trainer's 130-horsepower Gipsy Major engine gave it enough power that it was used for towing gliders at British soaring clubs for decades.
- Auster won export orders for the type to several Commonwealth countries, and a handful of Aiglets ended up flying as training airplanes for the Egyptian Air Force in the 1950s.
- The Aiglet Trainer was one of the last new designs from Auster before the company merged into Beagle Aircraft in 1960, effectively ending British light aircraft manufacturing as a domestic industry.
- A small number of restored Aiglet Trainers still fly today out of UK grass strips, mostly belonging to vintage aviation enthusiasts who restore them as living examples of postwar British design.

## Seed paragraph

Postwar Britain is broke, the empire is shrinking, and a small factory in Leicestershire is still trying to build a four-seat training airplane that doesn't look like an American knockoff. The Auster Aiglet Trainer is what they delivered — a steel-tube, fabric-covered taildragger with a 130-horsepower Gipsy Major up front. It towed gliders, trained Egyptian air force pilots and quietly carried the last decade of independent British light aircraft manufacturing on its fabric-covered shoulders.
