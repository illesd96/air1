# auster-autocar
## Hooks
- The Auster Autocar was Britain's answer to the postwar American light aircraft — a four-seat high-wing taildragger built at the Rearsby factory in Leicestershire from 1949 onward.
- Auster Aircraft began life as the British licensee for the Taylorcraft, the same airframe that became the American Piper Cub's contemporary — so the Autocar shared family DNA with the J-3.
- Royal Air Force air observation post units flew Auster variants in Korea, Malaya and Suez, making the Autocar's siblings the unsung British counterparts to the American L-4 Cub.
- Only about 28 Autocars were built, making it one of the rarer postwar British four-seaters and a coveted collector item at British vintage fly-ins.
- Auster Aircraft itself merged into Beagle Aircraft in 1960, ending a thirty-year run of distinctly British high-wing taildraggers that taught generations of RAF pilots their first lessons.

## Seed paragraph

Britain in 1949. The Spitfire factories are closed, petrol is rationed and the postwar dream of personal aviation looks more like American daydreaming than English practicality. But in a brick factory in Leicestershire, a small firm named Auster is still building four-seat fabric-covered high-wing taildraggers — the Autocar — for doctors and farmers who can scrape together the cash. Only 28 were ever finished. The ones still flying today are flown by people who very deliberately do not want to fly anything modern.
