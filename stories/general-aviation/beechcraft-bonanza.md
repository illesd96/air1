# beechcraft-bonanza
## Hooks
- On February 3, 1959, a Bonanza Model 35 piloted by 21-year-old Roger Peterson crashed in an Iowa cornfield, killing Buddy Holly, Ritchie Valens and the Big Bopper — the night Don McLean would later call 'the day the music died.'
- The Bonanza's distinctive V-tail earned it the nickname 'doctor killer' after a string of in-flight breakups in the 1980s among wealthy professional pilots, prompting an AD requiring metal cuffs on the tail.
- The Bonanza has been in continuous production since 1947, longer than any other airplane in history, with the same basic airframe still rolling out of Wichita today.
- Test pilot Vern Carstens flew the prototype from Wichita to the East Coast and back during 1947 to prove the new design, helping launch the postwar GA boom almost single-handedly.
- More than 18,000 Bonanzas have been built and a thriving community called the American Bonanza Society holds annual fly-ins where pilots compare 70-year-old airframes still in active service.

## Seed paragraph

A snowstorm. A cornfield outside Clear Lake, Iowa. A 21-year-old pilot with insufficient instrument training takes off into the night with three rock and roll stars on board. Five miles later the V-tail Bonanza augers into the snow at full power, killing everyone. The airplane Beechcraft built to sell the postwar dream of personal aviation would forever be linked to the day the music died.
