# brm-land-africa
## Hooks
- The BRM Land Africa is a Portuguese ultralight specifically designed for bush flying in Africa, with high-flotation tundra-style tires, a high wing and rugged tube-and-fabric construction.
- BRM Costruções Aeronáuticas is one of Portugal's few aircraft manufacturers, working out of a small facility outside Lisbon and exporting kits across Europe and the Lusophone world.
- The 'Land Africa' name is a direct nod to its intended customer — small operators in former Portuguese colonies like Angola and Mozambique who needed an airplane that could land on dirt tracks.
- It uses the Rotax 912 engine, the same powerplant in almost every modern European ultralight, giving it 100 horsepower for short-field takeoffs from grass and gravel.
- BRM markets the airplane both as a complete factory-built aircraft and as a kit, with builders typically completing the kit version in 500 to 700 hours of garage work.

## Seed paragraph

Outside Lisbon, in a small Portuguese factory, a tiny aerospace company called BRM builds an airplane specifically for the dust strips of southern Africa. They named it Land Africa. With a Rotax engine and tundra tires, it slips into Angolan villages and Mozambique cattle stations where 172s would tear up their nose gear. Portugal lost its empire in 1975 — but a quiet aviation thread between Lisbon and the old colonies is still flying, one ultralight at a time.
