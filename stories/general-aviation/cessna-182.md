# cessna-182
## Hooks
- The 182 Skylane is the 172's burlier sibling — same shape, but with a 230-horsepower engine, constant-speed prop, and enough useful load to actually fill all four seats with adults plus baggage.
- Civil Air Patrol uses the 182 as its primary search-and-rescue platform in the United States, flying grid patterns over disaster zones with onboard infrared cameras.
- Skylanes are the unofficial standard for the skydiving industry's small-operator fleet, hauling four-jumper loads up to 10,000 feet thousands of times per year per airframe.
- Cessna restarted 182 production in 1996 after a decade-long shutdown caused by liability lawsuits, the same break that froze the entire piston single industry.
- In 2008 a 182 became one of the first GA airplanes to fly across the U.S. on the new Garmin G1000 glass cockpit, marking the transition from steam gauges to fully integrated avionics in the personal airplane segment.

## Seed paragraph

If the Cessna 172 is how you learn to fly, the 182 Skylane is what you buy when you actually need to go somewhere. Bigger engine, constant-speed prop, real useful load. Civil Air Patrol flies it in disaster zones, skydive operators thrash it daily, and weekend pilots load four humans and a camping trip into one. It is the family minivan of the sky, and like a minivan, the people who own one swear they will never go back.
