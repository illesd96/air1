# pilatus-pc-12
## Hooks
- The PC-12 single-engine turboprop is so reliable that the FAA grudgingly let it fly commercial passengers single-pilot at night IFR — something it generally only allows on twin-engine airplanes.
- The Royal Flying Doctor Service of Australia operates dozens of PC-12s as flying ambulances across the outback, often landing on dirt strips marked by car headlights.
- Drug smugglers love the PC-12 because it can land on a 2,500-foot dirt strip with a 9,000-pound payload, which is why the DEA tracks them obsessively along the U.S.-Mexico border.
- Switzerland's Pilatus markets the airplane with a giant rear cargo door big enough to swallow a snowmobile or a Formula Ford race car.
- Surf Air and JSX built entire scheduled airline business models around the PC-12, selling all-you-can-fly memberships and bypassing TSA checkpoints by sticking to general aviation terminals.

## Seed paragraph

Somewhere over the Australian outback at 2 a.m., a single white turboprop with a Royal Flying Doctor Service red cross on its tail descends toward a dirt road lit by the headlights of two farm trucks. A station hand has appendicitis. The airplane is a Pilatus PC-12, and across the world it has quietly become the most useful single-engine machine ever built — flying doctor, drug-smuggling tool, billionaire's chariot and bush commuter, all in one Swiss-built airframe.
