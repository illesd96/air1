# piper-cub
## Hooks
- The bright yellow J-3 Cub trained roughly 75 percent of all U.S. military pilots in World War II under the Civilian Pilot Training Program before they ever climbed into a warplane.
- The Cub's signature yellow paint scheme — officially Lock Haven Yellow — was chosen for visibility and is now so iconic that it's known generically in aviation as 'Cub Yellow.'
- Cubs were so cheap and simple that during D-Day artillery spotters in militarized L-4 Cub variants flew low over Normandy to call in shellfire on German positions.
- The Cub is so light it can take off in about 100 feet and land in less, making it the favorite airplane of Alaska bush pilots who routinely set down on glaciers and sandbars.
- A Cub has only one door, no electrical system in its original form, and is traditionally hand-propped to start — but it still has a cult following with thousands flying today.
- Piper built more than 19,000 J-3 Cubs, and a clean restored example today sells for more than it cost new, adjusted for any reasonable measure of inflation.

## Seed paragraph

Paint it yellow. Hand-prop the engine. Climb in through the single door, kick off the brakes and float into the sky at 40 miles per hour. The Piper J-3 Cub taught a generation of Americans to fly, then helped them win World War II, then became the soul of bush flying. Nine decades later, the yellow airplane in the grass field is still the picture every pilot draws when they imagine flying.
