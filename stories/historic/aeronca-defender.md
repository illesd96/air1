# aeronca-defender
## Hooks
- The Aeronca Defender was the civilian-pattern parent of the military L-3 Grasshopper, a primary trainer and observation aircraft used by the US Army Air Corps in WWII for artillery spotting and liaison work at speeds slow enough to fly under enemy radar.
- Built in Middletown, Ohio by the Aeronautical Corporation of America — Aeronca was the first American manufacturer to mass-produce light aircraft for the private pilot market and the Defender sold for under $1,500 in 1940, less than a Ford sedan.
- Defenders and their L-3 derivatives were so simple to operate that Civil Aeronautics Administration Civilian Pilot Training Program graduates could solo them after only 8 hours of dual instruction — the program produced over 400,000 wartime pilots using aircraft like the Defender.
- The Defender used a Continental A-65 engine of just 65 horsepower — less power than a modern lawnmower tractor — yet could cruise at 87 mph and land in less than 300 feet on grass, making it the practical truck of pre-war civil aviation.
- Aeronca's tandem-seat design was directly copied — or 'inspired by,' depending on who you asked — Piper's J-3 Cub layout; the two companies fought a long quiet rivalry through the 1930s and 40s, with the Cub eventually winning the popular memory and the Aeronca becoming the forgotten one.

## Seed paragraph

Before America had an air force big enough to fight a world war, it had Aeroncas. Tens of thousands of young men learned to fly in tandem-seat 65-horsepower trainers like the Defender through the Civilian Pilot Training Program — the obscure New Deal initiative that produced the pilots who would later crew B-17s and P-51s. The Defender was the truck of civil aviation. The Piper Cub got famous. The Aeronca built the same world and was forgotten.
