# airspeed-consul
## Hooks
- The Airspeed Consul wasn't a clean-sheet design — it was a 1946 conversion of surplus Airspeed Oxford military trainers that had glutted British airfields at war's end, with the gun turret removed and a cabin door cut into the side.
- Nevil Shute, the novelist who wrote A Town Like Alice and On the Beach, co-founded Airspeed Limited in 1931 and remained a director until 1938 — the Consul was effectively built by a company that a famous novelist had part-owned.
- The Consul filled a strange gap in the postwar British market: too small to be a real airliner, too big for private use, it was bought by air taxi firms, photographic survey companies, and ambulance services in 30 countries — a workhorse with no glamor.
- Operated by Skyways of London, BKS Air Transport, and the early Cyprus Airways, the Consul became the de facto regional aircraft of the British Empire's twilight years — flying mail and small cargo to colonies that would not be colonies for much longer.
- By the mid-1950s the de Havilland Dove had taken its market — by 1960 nearly every Consul had been scrapped, and only one airworthy example survives, a quiet end for one of the last aircraft to bear a name from the great age of British aviation.

## Seed paragraph

When the Second World War ended, Britain had thousands of surplus Airspeed Oxford trainers nobody needed and no money for new airliners. So Airspeed — a small company that the novelist Nevil Shute had co-founded back in 1931 — cut a passenger door into the side, removed the gun positions, and called it the Consul. It was nobody's dream aircraft. It carried the mail and the small parcels of an Empire in its last decade.
