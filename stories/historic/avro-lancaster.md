# avro-lancaster
## Hooks
- The Lancaster was born from the catastrophic failure of the twin-engine Avro Manchester — Roy Chadwick simply stretched the wings and bolted on four Rolls-Royce Merlins to fix the Manchester's lethally unreliable Vulture engines, and the result became Bomber Command's masterpiece.
- Operation Chastise on 16 May 1943 lost 8 of 19 Lancasters and 53 of 133 aircrew — a 40% loss rate in a single night — to breach two German dams; the 'bouncing bomb' Upkeep was a 9,250-lb spinning steel drum dropped at exactly 60 feet and 232 mph, calibrated by training crews to use two pieces of converging spotlights as a primitive altimeter.
- Lancaster crews flew at night without fighter escort, sitting on a fuel tank, an unpressurized aluminum tube, and 14,000 lbs of bombs; statistically a Bomber Command tour of 30 ops gave you a 44% chance of survival — worse odds than a WWI infantry officer.
- Barnes Wallis followed up Upkeep with two even larger Lancaster weapons: the 12,000-lb Tallboy and the 22,000-lb Grand Slam 'earthquake bomb,' a single one of which sank the Tirpitz in November 1944.
- Only two airworthy Lancasters survive — PA474 in England and FM213 'Vera' in Canada — and they shared the skies just once, in 2014, the first time two Lancasters had flown together since 1964.

## Seed paragraph

A 9,250-pound steel drum, spinning backwards at 500 rpm, released from a Lancaster flying 60 feet above the water of the Möhne reservoir on the night of 16 May 1943, bounced across the surface like a skipped stone and sank against the dam wall before its hydrostatic fuse detonated. Of the 133 Royal Air Force airmen who took off that night, 53 did not come back. The Lancaster started life as the failed Manchester. Roy Chadwick gave it four engines and it became the greatest bomber of the war.
