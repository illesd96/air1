# b-25
## Hooks
- Sixteen B-25Bs took off from the deck of the USS Hornet on 18 April 1942, 650 miles from Japan — none of them ever landed on a carrier, none of them had ever practiced a carrier launch with a full bomb load, and only one of the 16 (which diverted to neutral Vladivostok) was recovered intact.
- Doolittle's raiders modified each B-25 by removing the tail gun and replacing it with two black-painted broomsticks, because the real Bendix turret was too heavy to make the carrier takeoff possible.
- On 28 July 1945 a B-25D became the only aircraft ever to crash into the Empire State Building — Lt. Col. William Smith got lost in fog and slammed into the 79th floor at 200 mph, killing 14 and starting the largest fire in Manhattan history above the 78th floor.
- The B-25H 'gunship' variant carried a 75mm M4 cannon — the largest gun ever fitted to a production aircraft, the same cannon used in the M3 Sherman tank — and could fire armor-piercing shells from the nose at Japanese shipping.
- Of Doolittle's 80 raiders, three were executed by the Japanese army after capture, five died in crashes, and 64 made it to Free China — Jimmy Doolittle himself initially expected to be court-martialed for losing every aircraft, instead he received the Medal of Honor.

## Seed paragraph

On the morning of 18 April 1942, sixteen North American B-25 medium bombers — designed for runways, never tested from a ship — accelerated off the rolling deck of the USS Hornet 650 miles from Tokyo. They had broomsticks for tail guns because the real ones were too heavy. None of them had enough fuel to land anywhere safe. Jimmy Doolittle expected to be court-martialed when it was over; instead, he got the Medal of Honor.
