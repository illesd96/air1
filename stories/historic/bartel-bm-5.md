# bartel-bm-5
## Hooks
- The Bartel BM 5 was Poland's first indigenous-designed advanced trainer — built at the Samolot factory in Poznań from 1928, it carried the Polish Air Force through the entire interwar pilot pipeline that would produce the legendary 303 Squadron pilots of the Battle of Britain.
- Ryszard Bartel, the designer, had worked at the German Albatros works during WWI before returning to newly independent Poland — many of Poland's interwar designers were similarly trained in German aviation and turned their skills against Germany when 1939 came.
- The aircraft used a 220-hp Wright Whirlwind J-5 radial — the same engine that powered Lindbergh's Spirit of St. Louis across the Atlantic — bought in small numbers by Poland because the new republic could not yet build its own engines.
- Of the roughly 70 BM 5s built, almost all were destroyed in September 1939 — either on the ground by the Luftwaffe or in the chaos of the Polish retreat — and not a single complete original survives anywhere in the world today.
- The Polish pilots trained on the BM 5 in the 1930s formed the backbone of the squadrons that escaped to Romania, then France, then Britain in 1939-40 — Polish 303 Squadron, equipped with Hawker Hurricanes, would become the highest-scoring RAF squadron of the Battle of Britain.

## Seed paragraph

In 1928 a Polish designer named Ryszard Bartel, trained at Albatros in Germany during the previous war, sat down at the Samolot works in Poznań and drew the first homegrown advanced trainer of the newly resurrected Polish Republic. The Bartel BM 5 was unglamorous, slow, and forgotten. The pilots it trained, however, escaped Poland in September 1939, flew Hurricanes from English airfields a year later, and ran up the highest score of any squadron in the Battle of Britain.
