# bell-x-1
## Hooks
- Chuck Yeager broke two ribs falling off a horse two nights before the supersonic flight and didn't tell the Air Force — he could not pull the cockpit door closed alone, so flight engineer Jack Ridley sawed off a broom handle to give him the leverage to lock it.
- The X-1's shape — a .50 caliber bullet with wings — was chosen because Bell's engineers knew bullets stayed stable past Mach 1 from artillery research; the program ran in such secrecy that even the wives of the test pilots didn't know what the aircraft did.
- On October 14, 1947, Yeager hit Mach 1.06 at 43,000 feet over the Mojave; the sonic boom was the first ever produced deliberately by a human, and the Air Force kept the achievement classified until June 1948.
- The X-1 was rocket-powered with liquid oxygen and a fuel of water-and-ethanol; on the ground the LOX boiled off at such a rate that the aircraft had to be loaded inside its B-29 mothership while already airborne to avoid running out before launch.
- Bell's chief test pilot Slick Goodlin demanded a $150,000 bonus to attempt Mach 1; the Air Force balked, fired him, and assigned the job to a 24-year-old Captain on $283 a month — Charles E. Yeager.

## Seed paragraph

Two nights before he was scheduled to fly faster than sound, Chuck Yeager fell off a horse in the Mojave desert and broke two ribs. He couldn't lift his arm to close the cockpit door of the Bell X-1. So a flight engineer named Jack Ridley sawed off a broom handle, handed it to him, and said nothing to the Air Force. The next morning, October 14, 1947, the broom handle locked the door and a 24-year-old captain from West Virginia became the first man to hear his own sonic boom.
