# boeing-247
## Hooks
- The Boeing 247 of February 1933 was the first true modern airliner — all-metal stressed-skin, retractable gear, cantilever wings, autopilot, de-icing boots, and trim tabs — but Boeing's own parent company United Aircraft Corporation reserved the first 60 production aircraft for United Airlines, locking out every competitor.
- Locked out of buying 247s, TWA went to a small Californian builder named Donald Douglas and asked for something better — the DC-1, then the DC-2, then the DC-3 followed; Boeing's monopolistic decision handed Douglas the airliner market for the next 25 years.
- On 10 October 1933 a United 247 over Chesterton, Indiana became the first commercial airliner brought down by a deliberate bomb in flight — seven passengers and three crew died, and the case was never solved.
- The 247 carried only 10 passengers, while the DC-2 that followed it carried 14 and was 35 mph faster — Boeing had built the right aircraft, just one size too small, and the lesson reshaped commercial aviation specifications for the next two decades.
- United Aircraft Corporation was broken up by the 1934 Air Mail Act, with Boeing forced to divest its airline (which became United Airlines) and its engine maker (which became Pratt & Whitney) — the antitrust action triggered partly by the 247 monopoly forever changed the structure of American aviation.

## Seed paragraph

In February 1933 Boeing flew the world's first truly modern airliner — all metal, retractable gear, autopilot, the works — and then made the single greatest commercial blunder in aviation history. They told every airline except their own subsidiary, United, to wait. TWA stormed off, knocked on a small California door belonging to Donald Douglas, and asked him to build something better. He built the DC-3. Boeing wouldn't lead the airliner market again for twenty-five years.
