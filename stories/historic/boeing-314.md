# boeing-314
## Hooks
- The Boeing 314 had no precedent for transatlantic luxury — Pan Am passengers slept in proper berths with sheets, dined on Pullman china served by white-jacketed stewards, and could walk a 'promenade deck' inside the wing root at 11,000 feet over the open ocean.
- Only twelve were built, and one of them — the Dixie Clipper — flew President Franklin D. Roosevelt to the Casablanca Conference in January 1943, the first time a sitting US president had ever flown anywhere.
- When the Pacific Clipper found itself stranded in Auckland after Pearl Harbor closed its return route across the Pacific, Pan Am captain Robert Ford flew west around the world — Karachi, Khartoum, Léopoldville, Natal, New York — completing the first commercial round-the-world flight in 1942 by accident.
- The 314's gigantic vertical stabilizer was the third version Boeing tried; the first single fin had no rudder authority, the second twin-tail had flutter problems, and the final triple-tail design was developed only after a near-disaster on the prototype's maiden flight.
- Boeing built the 314 in only six months of actual production time, reusing the wing of the failed XB-15 bomber — a piece of engineering opportunism that gave Pan Am the longest-ranged airliner in the world from a single-piece wing originally intended to drop bombs on Tokyo.

## Seed paragraph

When Pearl Harbor was attacked, Pan Am Captain Robert Ford was in Auckland with a Boeing 314 flying boat and no way home — the Pacific route had become a war zone overnight. So he flew west. Karachi, Khartoum, Léopoldville, Natal, then up to New York. By the time the Pacific Clipper splashed down in the East River in January 1942, he had become the first commercial pilot to circle the Earth. Six months earlier he had been carrying honeymooners to Honolulu.
