# de-havilland-comet
## Hooks
- Two Comet 1s broke apart in mid-air over the Mediterranean in 1954 — BOAC Flight 781 in January and South African Flight 201 in April — both at exactly 30,000 feet, and the cause turned out to be metal fatigue around the corners of the square cabin windows after just 1,290 and 900 pressurization cycles respectively.
- The Royal Navy did something never done before or since for an air accident: they dredged most of Flight 781 from 600 feet of Mediterranean seawater and reassembled the fuselage in a hangar; the cracks radiated unambiguously from a window corner that had failed in metal fatigue.
- De Havilland had pressure-tested the Comet's fuselage to twice operating pressure and seen no failures — what they didn't account for was that real-world pressurization cycles, expanding and contracting the aluminum tens of thousands of times, would propagate cracks invisible to inspection.
- The Comet 4 returned in 1958 with oval windows and a thicker skin — and it was beaten to the first transatlantic jet service by Pan Am's Boeing 707 by three weeks, a lead the British civil aviation industry never recovered.
- Hawker Siddeley repurposed the Comet 4 design as the Nimrod maritime patrol aircraft, which the RAF kept flying until 2011 — meaning the Comet's airframe outlived the British jet airliner industry it had failed to save.

## Seed paragraph

The Royal Navy dredged the Comet up from the bottom of the Mediterranean in pieces, laid the fragments out on a hangar floor in Farnborough, and watched the truth emerge: it was the corners of the square windows. Aluminum, fatigued by thousands of pressurization cycles invisible to the naked eye, had finally split open at 30,000 feet over Elba. Britain had invented the jet airliner. Britain then handed it to Boeing.
