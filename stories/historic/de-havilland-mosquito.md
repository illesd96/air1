# de-havilland-mosquito
## Hooks
- The 'Wooden Wonder' was rejected by the Air Ministry three times — they wanted defensive turrets, multiple crew, and a bomb bay; Geoffrey de Havilland built it anyway in 1940 with no guns, two crew, and a fuselage of spruce-balsa-spruce plywood sandwich, and at first flight it was 20 mph faster than the Spitfire.
- The Mosquito's wooden construction wasn't sentimental — it was wartime ingenuity: it used Britain's furniture industry, not its strategic aluminum supply, and assembly was farmed out to over 400 small workshops including piano factories, casket makers, and a coffin manufacturer in High Wycombe.
- On 30 January 1943 four Mosquitos interrupted Hermann Göring's live radio speech celebrating the 10th anniversary of Nazi rule by bombing Berlin at lunchtime — Göring later said: 'It makes me furious when I see the Mosquito; the British... knock together a beautiful wooden aircraft that every piano factory over there is building.'
- The Pathfinder Force Mosquitos marked targets for Bomber Command's Lancasters with such precision that target accuracy improved by an order of magnitude; one veteran target marker, Gp Cpt Hamish Mahaddie, dropped flares within 100 yards of his aim point at night from 4,000 feet without a radar.
- The Mosquito FB Mk VI of 487 Squadron flew Operation Jericho on 18 February 1944, breaching the walls of Amiens prison at rooftop level to release 258 French Resistance prisoners hours before their scheduled execution — only one wall was hit, but exactly the right wall.

## Seed paragraph

The Air Ministry told Geoffrey de Havilland three times to stop. They wanted defensive turrets. They wanted four crew. They wanted aluminum. He ignored them and built a bomber out of plywood, spruce, and balsa wood — assembled by piano factories and coffin makers across the Home Counties — that flew 20 miles per hour faster than the Spitfire and had no guns at all. Hermann Göring went on record saying it made him furious.
