# douglas-dc-3
## Hooks
- The DC-3's military designation C-47 'Skytrain' — and its RAF name 'Dakota' — became Eisenhower's pick alongside the bazooka, jeep, and atom bomb as the four pieces of equipment that won WWII; over 10,000 were built and they dropped 13,000 paratroopers into Normandy on D-Day alone.
- C-47s flew 'the Hump' from India to China over the Himalayas, the most dangerous airlift in history — between 1942 and 1945 over 600 transports were lost on the route, the wreckage so dense pilots navigated by following the 'aluminum trail' of crashed predecessors.
- Over 400 DC-3/C-47 airframes were still in commercial revenue service in 2024, almost 90 years after the type first flew — Buffalo Airways in Canada flew them daily on freight runs until 2022, and the South African Air Force operated them into the 2020s.
- The DC-3 was built so robustly that during Operation Market Garden in 1944, C-47s flew low and slow over German antiaircraft fire, took dozens of 20mm hits per airframe, and kept their paratroopers in formation — its structural margin was rumored to be 4x specification because Donald Douglas built it to airline standards, not war-economy.
- Lisunov Li-2 was the Soviet license-built DC-3 (with a Russian engine and metric fasteners); Showa L2D was the Japanese license-built DC-3 — meaning every major combatant in WWII operated some variant of the same American airliner.

## Seed paragraph

Eisenhower listed it alongside the bazooka, the jeep, and the atomic bomb as the four pieces of equipment that won the Second World War. The Soviets built it under license. The Japanese built it under license. The Germans captured them and flew them. Ninety years after its first flight, hundreds of DC-3s are still earning a living somewhere — a piston-engined transport designed in 1934 that simply refuses to be retired.
