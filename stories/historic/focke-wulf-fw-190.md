# focke-wulf-fw-190
## Hooks
- When the Fw 190 first appeared over the Channel in August 1941 it outclassed every Spitfire variant so badly the RAF assumed Germany had stolen a Hawker Tornado prototype — and they had no answer until a German pilot named Armin Faber accidentally landed an Fw 190A-3 at RAF Pembrey in June 1942, thinking it was an airfield in France.
- Kurt Tank chose an air-cooled BMW 801 radial when every other major fighter designer of the era was building inline V12s — he argued radials could absorb battle damage and operate without a vulnerable cooling system, and he was right.
- The Fw 190 was Tank's first single-engine fighter; he was a glider designer before the war and named the long-nose 'Ta 152' high-altitude variant after himself when Hitler's RLM finally let him.
- Otto Kittel and Walter Nowotny both scored over 250 victories in Fw 190s on the Eastern Front; the type was a brutal close-range brawler that could survive hits that would shred a Bf 109.
- The 'Sturmböcke' (battering-ram) variants of late 1944 were so heavily armored they were designed to ram US heavy bombers from behind, the pilot bailing out at the last moment — a tactic that killed pilots faster than it killed B-17s.

## Seed paragraph

On the morning of 23 June 1942, an exhausted Luftwaffe pilot named Armin Faber, disoriented after a dogfight, mistook the Bristol Channel for the English Channel and landed his brand-new Fw 190A-3 at RAF Pembrey in Wales. The Royal Air Force had been desperate for nearly a year to learn how Kurt Tank had built a fighter that outclassed every Spitfire in service. Faber's mistake handed them one with full fuel and ammunition.
