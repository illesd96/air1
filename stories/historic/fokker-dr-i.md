# fokker-dr-i
## Hooks
- Manfred von Richthofen scored only 19 of his 80 victories in the Fokker Dr.I — the vast majority came in the Albatros D.II, D.III, and D.V; the triplane is famous because it was painted red and he died in it, not because it was his best aircraft.
- The Red Baron was almost certainly killed on 21 April 1918 by a single .303 round fired from the ground, not by Canadian Sopwith Camel pilot Roy Brown as the RAF officially credited — modern forensic analysis points to Australian Lewis gunners on a ridge near the Somme.
- Anthony Fokker copied the triplane concept directly from a captured Sopwith Triplane after Werner Voss and others marveled at the British design; the Dr.I was reverse-engineered from RFC enemy hardware.
- Wing failures killed at least 11 Dr.I pilots in late 1917 — moisture seeped into improperly varnished wing ribs and the cellulose adhesive disintegrated, causing top wings to separate in dives; the type was grounded for inspections before Richthofen's death.
- Only 320 Dr.Is were ever built and not one original example survives — every triplane in a museum today is a replica, including the one in the Smithsonian, because the last authentic Dr.I was destroyed in the RAF Museum bombing in 1945.

## Seed paragraph

Everyone remembers the red triplane. Almost everyone gets the story wrong. Manfred von Richthofen scored more than three-quarters of his 80 kills in the Albatros, not the Fokker Dr.I; the British credited Roy Brown with shooting him down but the bullet that killed him almost certainly came from an Australian machine-gunner on the ground; and the design itself was reverse-engineered from a captured Sopwith. Not one original Dr.I survives — every triplane in the world today is a copy.
