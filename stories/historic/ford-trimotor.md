# ford-trimotor
## Hooks
- Henry Ford's son Edsel pushed his father into aviation — Henry Ford himself disliked airplanes and thought they were a passing fashion; the Trimotor was based on an aluminum airliner designed by William Stout, whom Ford bought out in 1925 mostly to keep Edsel happy.
- Admiral Richard Byrd flew a Ford Trimotor named 'Floyd Bennett' to the first crossing of the South Pole on 28-29 November 1929 — the aircraft barely cleared the polar plateau at 11,000 feet and had to jettison emergency food to gain altitude over the last ridge.
- The 'Tin Goose' was the world's first all-metal multi-engine airliner in serial production, but it was almost identical externally to the Junkers F.13 — Ford's engineers were accused of and quietly settled charges of patent infringement with Hugo Junkers over the corrugated aluminum skin.
- American Airlines, TWA, and Pan Am all started revenue service with Ford Trimotors; they were the workhorse of the very first US scheduled passenger network, but their unpressurized cruise at 5,000 feet through every weather system led passengers to dub the experience 'the Ford rollercoaster.'
- Production stopped abruptly in 1933 — Ford lost interest after his test pilot and friend Harry Brooks died in a Ford Flivver experimental aircraft crash, and he refused to authorize the modernization the Trimotor needed; the Boeing 247 and DC-2 made it obsolete inside two years.

## Seed paragraph

Henry Ford did not actually like airplanes. His son Edsel did. So Edsel cajoled his father into buying out the aluminum-airliner designer William Stout in 1925, and the result — the corrugated, three-engined Tin Goose — became the first true American airliner. Admiral Byrd flew one over the South Pole. Then Ford's personal test pilot died in a different aircraft, and Henry, grief-stricken, simply stopped building the Trimotor in 1933 while the Boeing 247 and DC-2 were eating its lunch.
