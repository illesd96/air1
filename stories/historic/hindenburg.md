# hindenburg
## Hooks
- The Hindenburg was designed from the start to use helium, but the US Helium Control Act of 1927 and Interior Secretary Harold Ickes's personal refusal to license export to Nazi Germany forced Hugo Eckener to refill the gas cells with hydrogen — the same flammable gas that turned Lakehurst into the most-replayed disaster newsreel of the 1930s.
- Of the 97 people aboard, 62 survived — the Hindenburg disaster killed only 35 onboard plus one ground crewman, fewer than die in a typical airliner crash, but the simultaneous radio broadcast by Herbert Morrison and the newsreel cameras made it the first global media tragedy.
- The leading modern theory, championed by retired NASA scientist Addison Bain, is that the doped cotton outer envelope — impregnated with iron oxide and aluminum powder, essentially thermite paint — caught fire from static discharge in a thunderstorm before any hydrogen ignited.
- Hindenburg passengers flew Frankfurt to Lakehurst in 2.5 days for $400 (about $9,000 today), ate on Bavarian china in a dining room with grand piano, and could smoke in a pressurized smoking lounge with a single electric lighter chained to the wall.
- Captain Max Pruss, badly burned in the crash, survived and lived until 1960, insisting until his death that the Hindenburg had been sabotaged — a theory the FBI investigated and quietly dropped after finding no evidence.

## Seed paragraph

She was meant to fly on helium. She flew on hydrogen because the United States Congress, fearing the gas might end up in Luftwaffe airships, refused to sell it to Nazi Germany. On May 6, 1937, the most luxurious flying machine ever built drifted into a mooring mast at Lakehurst, New Jersey, and burned to her aluminum bones in 37 seconds while Herbert Morrison wept into a live microphone.
