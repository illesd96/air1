# howard-hughes-h-4
## Hooks
- Hughes built the Spruce Goose almost entirely of birch plywood, not spruce, but the slur 'Spruce Goose' — coined by a hostile press during the 1947 Senate hearings — stuck because it rhymed; Hughes hated the name so much he refused to use it for the rest of his life.
- The H-4 flew exactly once, on November 2, 1947, lifting maybe 70 feet over Long Beach Harbor for about a mile, three days after Hughes had sworn to Senator Owen Brewster's committee that the aircraft would fly — many believe Hughes never intended to fly it at all until the political pressure made it unavoidable.
- The 320-foot wingspan stood as the world record until Stratolaunch's 'Roc' first flew in 2019 — for 72 years no aircraft had wider wings than a wooden flying boat that left the water once.
- Hughes spent $23 million of his own money plus $18 million of government funds keeping the H-4 program alive after WWII ended; the entire airframe was kept climate-controlled in a hangar at $1 million a year until his death in 1976, with one mechanic permanently assigned to wipe down the wings.
- The 'Duramold' laminated birch construction technique was developed because wartime aluminum was rationed for combat aircraft — by the time the H-4 was ready in 1947 the war had been over for two years and aluminum was abundant, making the entire wooden premise obsolete.

## Seed paragraph

Howard Hughes spent eight years and forty-one million dollars building a wooden flying boat the size of a city block, then flew it once, for one mile, at seventy feet, in front of a Senate committee that was trying to send him to prison for war profiteering. He kept the aircraft in a climate-controlled hangar for the next twenty-nine years and never let anyone touch it. He called it the Hercules. Everyone else called it the Spruce Goose, even though it was made of birch.
