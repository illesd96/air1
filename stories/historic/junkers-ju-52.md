# junkers-ju-52
## Hooks
- The corrugated duralumin skin that gave the 'Tante Ju' its washboard appearance was a Hugo Junkers patent dating to 1915 — the corrugations added longitudinal strength without internal stiffeners, but they cost the Ju 52 an estimated 40 km/h in cruise speed versus a smooth-skinned equivalent.
- Junkers himself was a pacifist socialist; the Nazis stripped him of his patents and his company in 1933, placed him under house arrest, and he died in 1935 — his transport aircraft then carried the Wehrmacht into Norway, the Low Countries, and Crete without him.
- On the first day of Operation Mercury (the airborne invasion of Crete, 20 May 1941), the Luftwaffe lost 151 Ju 52s — so devastating that Hitler personally banned major airborne operations for the rest of the war, killing the German paratroop concept Kurt Student had pioneered.
- The Ju 52 was the primary aircraft of the Stalingrad airlift in winter 1942-43; trying to supply 250,000 trapped 6th Army soldiers, the Luftwaffe lost 266 Ju 52s to weather and Soviet fighters and still failed to deliver even a third of the daily tonnage required.
- France continued to build the Ju 52 after the war as the AAC.1 'Toucan', and Spain as the CASA 352 — French Toucans dropped paratroopers in Indochina against the Viet Minh until 1954, making the Tante Ju a combat aircraft on four continents over three decades.

## Seed paragraph

Hugo Junkers was a pacifist who hated the Nazis. They put him under house arrest in 1933, stripped him of his patents, and he died two years later. His corrugated-aluminum trimotor airliner then carried Wehrmacht paratroopers into Norway, Holland, and Crete — and a hundred and fifty-one of them crashed on the first day over Crete alone. Hitler banned mass airborne operations forever. The Tante Ju kept flying anyway, in French colors, into the Indochina war.
