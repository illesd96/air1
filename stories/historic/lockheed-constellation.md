# lockheed-constellation
## Hooks
- The Constellation was designed in secret by Kelly Johnson at the personal demand of Howard Hughes, then the controlling shareholder of TWA — Hughes wrote the specifications on a napkin in 1939 and forbade Lockheed from showing the aircraft to anyone else until 1944.
- Its triple tail wasn't an aesthetic choice — it was forced by the height of TWA's existing hangars, which couldn't accommodate a single tall fin; Kelly Johnson split the rudder into three smaller fins to keep the aircraft hangar-compatible.
- The Constellation was the first pressurized airliner family in widespread use, flying above weather at 25,000 feet when contemporary DC-4s and DC-6s rattled through it — but the Wright R-3350 Turbo-Compound engines that gave it the range were so unreliable that crews called them 'flying time bombs.'
- President Eisenhower's personal Constellation, 'Columbine III', was the first official aircraft to use the radio call sign 'Air Force One' after a 1953 near-miss with a commercial Eastern Air Lines Constellation in the same airspace.
- The Constellation was killed by the very company that built it — Lockheed's own L-188 Electra and then the Boeing 707 made piston airliners obsolete; the last Connie scheduled service ended in 1967, just 22 years after she first flew passengers.

## Seed paragraph

Howard Hughes scribbled the specifications on a napkin in 1939 and ordered Kelly Johnson, the same Skunk Works designer who would later build the U-2 and the SR-71, to build him the most glamorous airliner in the world — in secret. The triple tail had nothing to do with style; it existed because TWA's hangars weren't tall enough for a single fin. The result defined what jet-age luxury would look like before there were any jets.
