# sopwith-camel
## Hooks
- The Camel got its name from the metal hump over its twin Vickers guns — pilots joked the hump and the bite gave it a camel's personality, since the rotary engine's gyroscopic effect made it turn so violently to the right it could spin off in a takeoff stall.
- Camels shot down 1,294 enemy aircraft — more than any other Allied fighter of the war — but killed an estimated 385 of their own pilots in training accidents before they ever saw the Western Front, more than any other British aircraft of WWI.
- The Camel was the first British fighter with two synchronized forward-firing machine guns, the first to use the new Constantinesco interrupter gear that fired bullets between propeller blades hydraulically, not mechanically.
- On 21 April 1918, Canadian Captain Roy Brown was officially credited with shooting down Manfred von Richthofen in a Camel, though modern forensic analysis points to an Australian ground gunner — Brown spent the rest of his life ambivalent about a kill he wasn't sure was his.
- The Sopwith Camel's torque was so vicious that veteran pilots took off with full right rudder; an unmanaged takeoff at low altitude could flip the aircraft inverted before the wheels left the grass, and the term 'gone west' originated as Camel slang for spinning fatally to the right.

## Seed paragraph

Snoopy's plane killed more Englishmen than Germans. The Sopwith Camel's vicious rotary-engine torque spun off so many trainees in 1917 that the Royal Flying Corps lost an estimated 385 pilots before they ever saw the Front. The same lethal handling, in the hands of a master, made it the war's deadliest fighter — credited with downing 1,294 enemy aircraft, including, officially, the Red Baron himself. Modern historians aren't so sure about the Red Baron part.
