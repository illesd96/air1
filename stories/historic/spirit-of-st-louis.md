# spirit-of-st-louis
## Hooks
- Lindbergh was the 19th person to fly the Atlantic nonstop — Alcock and Brown had done it in 1919, eight years earlier — but he was the first to do it solo and the first to fly New York to Paris, the specific route required to claim Raymond Orteig's $25,000 prize.
- Lindbergh deliberately built the Spirit with no front windshield — only a periscope — because Ryan Aircraft put the main fuel tank where his eyes would have been; he flew 33.5 hours seeing the world only through a periscope and side windows.
- He removed his radio, his parachute, his fuel gauge, and even cut the margins off his maps to save weight — and stayed awake by holding his eyelids open with his fingers and slapping himself while flying inches off the wave tops over the Atlantic.
- Six men had died trying to win the Orteig Prize in the weeks before Lindbergh took off; he beat the favored French ace Charles Nungesser, whose Levasseur biplane disappeared without trace over the Atlantic two weeks before Lindbergh's flight.
- The aircraft was built in 60 days by 35 Ryan Aircraft employees in San Diego for $10,580; Lindbergh paid for it himself with money from St. Louis businessmen, hence the name — they had no idea their investment would make him the most famous human being on Earth.

## Seed paragraph

Six men had died trying to win the Orteig Prize. The favored French ace Charles Nungesser had vanished over the Atlantic two weeks before. So a 25-year-old US Air Mail pilot from Minnesota, financed by St. Louis businessmen, walked into Ryan Aircraft in San Diego and ordered a custom monoplane with no front window. Sixty days later he flew it 33.5 hours from Long Island to Le Bourget, alone, and became the most famous human being alive.
