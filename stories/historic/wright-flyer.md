# wright-flyer
## Hooks
- The Wrights almost lost the patent war and the prize: Gustave Whitehead, Alberto Santos-Dumont, and Samuel Langley all had partisans claiming they flew first, and Langley's Aerodrome had crashed into the Potomac just nine days before Kitty Hawk while the Wrights were funded entirely out of their own bicycle-shop pocket.
- The famous photograph of the first flight exists only because John T. Daniels, a Kill Devil Hills lifeguard who had never operated a camera, squeezed the bulb at Orville's instruction — and then admitted he was so excited he couldn't remember if he'd actually pressed it.
- The Flyer's wing-warping control was reverse-engineered from watching turkey vultures bank near the Wrights' bicycle shop, and the engine block was hand-cast by their mechanic Charlie Taylor in six weeks because no manufacturer would build them one light enough.
- After the fourth flight that day a gust flipped the Flyer and wrecked it — it never flew again, and the Wrights then spent five frustrating years being dismissed as cranks by the US Army and the European press, who refused to believe two Ohio bicycle mechanics had beaten the world.
- The Smithsonian refused to display the Flyer for decades because it had endorsed Langley's Aerodrome as the first capable machine, prompting Orville to ship the aircraft to London — it only came home in 1948, one year after Orville's death.

## Seed paragraph

Two brothers who fixed bicycles for a living, working from a windswept dune in North Carolina because their hometown of Dayton, Ohio had no wind and no privacy, beat the Smithsonian-funded Samuel Langley by nine days and a hundred thousand dollars. For the next five years almost nobody believed them. The first flight lasted twelve seconds and traveled less than the wingspan of a 747.
