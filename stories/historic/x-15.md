# x-15
## Hooks
- Eight X-15 pilots qualified for astronaut wings by flying above 50 miles, but only one — Joseph Walker — crossed the 100-kilometer Kármán line, twice, in 1963; NASA didn't recognize the Air Force's 50-mile line until 2005, retroactively giving wings to pilots who'd been dead for decades.
- Neil Armstrong nearly ended Apollo before it started: in 1962 he bounced his X-15 off the atmosphere at 207,500 feet and 'skipped' 45 miles past Edwards in a powerless glide, almost overflying Los Angeles before landing dry-lake-bed-short at Mud Lake.
- The X-15 was launched from under the wing of a B-52 because no runway could survive its rocket exhaust and no fuel tank could hold enough anhydrous ammonia for both takeoff and a useful flight.
- Mike Adams was killed in 1967 when his X-15 entered a hypersonic spin at Mach 5 — the airframe disintegrated at over 4,000 mph and the cockpit was found 60 miles from where it broke up; he became NASA's first in-flight fatality.
- The X-15's speed record of Mach 6.7 (4,520 mph), set by William 'Pete' Knight in 1967, has never been beaten by any crewed, winged, air-breathing or rocket aircraft — the Space Shuttle was faster only in orbit, not in atmosphere.

## Seed paragraph

Before he stepped onto the Moon, Neil Armstrong skipped an experimental rocket plane across the top of the atmosphere and almost overflew Los Angeles in a powerless glide. The X-15 wasn't a spacecraft and it wasn't really an airplane — it was a black needle of Inconel-X dropped from a B-52 at 45,000 feet, lit with anhydrous ammonia, and pointed at the edge of space. Twelve men flew it. One of them died at Mach 5.
