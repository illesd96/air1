# zeppelin
## Hooks
- The Graf Zeppelin LZ 127 circumnavigated the globe in August 1929 — 21 days, 49,618 km, four stops — sponsored largely by William Randolph Hearst, who put up half the cost in exchange for exclusive press rights and required the journey to officially begin and end at Lakehurst, New Jersey.
- The LZ 127 was named after Count Ferdinand von Zeppelin, who had died in 1917 having invented the rigid airship after his own retirement from the Württemberg army at age 52 — every Zeppelin he saw fly was a vindication of an idea his colleagues had called the obsession of a deranged old aristocrat.
- Unlike the later Hindenburg, the Graf Zeppelin's seven engines burned 'Blaugas' — a propane-butane mix with the same density as air, stored in separate cells, so that burning fuel didn't make the airship buoyant and require venting hydrogen.
- The Graf Zeppelin flew more than a million miles in commercial service between 1928 and 1937 — including 64 Atlantic crossings, a polar expedition with George Wilkins, and regular runs to Brazil — carrying 13,110 passengers without losing a single one.
- Hugo Eckener, the LZ 127's chief pilot and commander, was anti-Nazi and refused to fly the Hitler salute; in 1936 the regime forced him to retire to obscurity, and the Graf Zeppelin was grounded forever after the Hindenburg disaster the following year.

## Seed paragraph

William Randolph Hearst paid half the cost of the Graf Zeppelin's 1929 round-the-world flight in exchange for press exclusivity, on one condition: the journey had to start and finish at Lakehurst, New Jersey, even though the airship was German. Twenty-one days and 49,000 kilometers later, the LZ 127 had become the first aircraft to circle the earth on a passenger schedule — and would go on to fly a million miles before the Hindenburg's flames ended the entire airship age.
