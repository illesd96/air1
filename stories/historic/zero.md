# zero
## Hooks
- The Zero's legendary 12-G maneuverability came from a single, brutal engineering decision by Jiro Horikoshi: no armor, no self-sealing fuel tanks, and a fuselage skin so thin a screwdriver could puncture it — pilots called it 'a flying Zippo.'
- On June 4, 1942, Petty Officer Tadayoshi Koga's Zero made a forced landing on Akutan Island in the Aleutians with its pilot dead at the controls; Americans recovered it almost intact, and the captured 'Akutan Zero' taught US Navy engineers exactly how to build the Hellcat that would kill the type wholesale.
- Saburō Sakai flew 200 miles back to Rabaul one-eyed, half-paralyzed, and with a piece of skull missing after being shot in the head by a Dauntless gunner over Guadalcanal — he flew Zeros until the surrender and only died in 2000.
- The Mitsubishi factory in Nagoya, which built most of the 10,939 Zeros made, had no roads wide enough to transport finished aircraft, so completed Zeros were pulled by oxen 30 miles to the airfield until 1944.
- The kamikaze role the Zero became famous for in 1944 was never part of its design — by then it was hopelessly outclassed by the F6F Hellcat and F4U Corsair, and pilots with less than 50 hours of training were sent on one-way missions because Japan had no fuel left to bring them home.

## Seed paragraph

On a fogbound island in the Aleutians in June 1942, a downed Mitsubishi A6M came to rest in soft tundra with its dead pilot still strapped in. The Americans crated it back to San Diego, fixed the propeller, and flew it against every fighter the US Navy had. Six months later they knew exactly how to kill it — and the same lightness that made the Zero a god over Pearl Harbor made it a coffin over the Marianas.
