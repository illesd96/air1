# ah-64
## Hooks
- The first shots of the 1991 Gulf War were fired by AH-64 Apaches in Task Force Normandy, which destroyed two Iraqi early-warning radars at 02:38 on January 17 — opening a corridor for the entire air campaign.
- The Apache's Longbow radar can detect 256 targets simultaneously, prioritize the 16 most dangerous, and engage them all without exposing the aircraft above terrain cover.
- An Apache pilot named Chad Bardwell survived an RPG hit to his helicopter's transmission over Najaf in 2003 by autorotating onto a freeway during rush hour traffic.
- British AH-64s during the 2011 Libya intervention were the first to launch combat operations from a Royal Navy ship — proving the Apache could be used as a naval asset.
- The Apache's 'IHADSS' helmet-mounted sight slaves the 30mm chain gun to wherever the gunner looks — the gun follows the eyeballs.
- AH-64 production has continued for 40 years and the airframe is now operated by 18 countries — including former Soviet states like Estonia.

## Seed paragraph

At 02:38 local time on January 17, 1991, two flights of Army Apache helicopters fired Hellfire missiles into two Iraqi radar stations along the Saudi border. They were the first shots of the Gulf War — and they opened the corridor through which the entire coalition air campaign would pour. Forty years after the Apache first flew, eighteen countries operate it, including Estonia. The gunner doesn't aim by looking at a screen. The 30mm cannon follows his eyeballs.
