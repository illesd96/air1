# b-17
## Hooks
- A B-17 named 'All American' made it home with its tail almost severed by a German fighter collision, held together by a thread of aluminum — the photograph became one of the most famous images of WWII.
- On April 14, 1945, Lt. John C. Morgan kept his B-17 in formation despite a head wound that left half his skull exposed; he later received the Medal of Honor.
- The 'Bloody Hundredth' bomb group lost 184 B-17s during the war — entire classes of crews disappeared in single missions, and superstitious aviators claimed the unit was cursed.
- B-17 ball turret gunners were the smallest men in the crew — often under 5'4" — and lay curled in a fetal position for entire missions with no parachute room, watching the world through a Plexiglas bubble.
- The legendary Memphis Belle wasn't actually the first B-17 to complete 25 missions — the Hell's Angels of the 303rd Bomb Group beat her to it, but the Belle had better PR.
- A captured B-17 was flown by the Luftwaffe under the codename 'KG 200,' used to infiltrate American bomber formations and report positions back to German fighter controllers.

## Seed paragraph

On February 1, 1943, a German Bf 109 sliced through the tail of an American B-17 called 'All American' — and somehow the bomber stayed in the air. The fuselage was held together by what looked like ribbons of aluminum. The crew flew it home through enemy fighter cover. The photograph went around the world. But the Flying Fortress wasn't just a tough airframe — it was a meat grinder, and the Bloody Hundredth Bomb Group lost so many crews that men began to whisper the unit was cursed.
