# b-29
## Hooks
- The B-29 program cost more than the Manhattan Project — $3 billion versus $1.9 billion — making it the most expensive weapons program of WWII.
- Three B-29s made forced landings in Soviet territory after raids on Japan; Stalin refused to return them and ordered Tupolev to copy them rivet by rivet, creating the Tu-4 'Bull.'
- The Superfortress had four remotely-controlled gun turrets operated through an early analog computer — the world's first fly-by-wire fire control system.
- Curtis LeMay stripped guns and armor from B-29s and sent them at low altitude over Tokyo on March 9-10, 1945, killing over 100,000 people in a single night — the deadliest air raid in history.
- The Enola Gay and Bockscar, the two B-29s that dropped the atomic bombs, both survive today — Enola Gay at the Smithsonian, Bockscar at the National Museum of the Air Force.
- B-29 production was so rushed that early aircraft routinely caught fire from overheating Wright R-3350 engines — more crews died in training accidents than in combat during 1944.

## Seed paragraph

It cost more than the atomic bomb that it carried. The B-29 Superfortress was a $3 billion gamble on aluminum, computers, and four engines that wouldn't stop catching fire. By the spring of 1945, the program had killed more American crews in training than in combat. Then it killed 100,000 people in Tokyo in a single night, and 200,000 more over Hiroshima and Nagasaki. And when three of them landed in Russia by mistake, Stalin copied every rivet.
