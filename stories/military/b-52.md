# b-52
## Hooks
- The B-52's original design was sketched in a Dayton hotel room over a single weekend in 1948 by a team of Boeing engineers who built a balsa-wood model to convince the Air Force.
- A B-52 accidentally dropped two hydrogen bombs on North Carolina in 1961 — one parachute deployed and the bomb was recovered intact, but the second is still buried in a swamp near Goldsboro.
- The Air Force has officially planned to fly the B-52 until at least 2050, meaning some airframes will be more than 90 years old when they finally retire.
- When the new Rolls-Royce F130 engines are installed in the 2030s, B-52 pilots will fly jets older than their grandfathers using engines built in the same year as their children.
- During the Vietnam War's Operation Linebacker II, B-52s flew so high that crews dropped bombs through cloud cover using radar — they never saw their targets, but Hanoi negotiated within eleven days.
- B-52 crews in the 1980s would sometimes spot UFOs on their radar during nuclear alert — declassified files show some of these turned out to be Soviet ferret satellites probing US airspace.

## Seed paragraph

On January 24, 1961, a B-52 broke apart over Goldsboro, North Carolina, and accidentally released two four-megaton hydrogen bombs. One landed in a field. The other is still down there, buried somewhere under a swamp the Air Force quietly bought from the family who owned it. The plane that dropped them is still flying today — and the Pentagon plans to keep it flying until 2050.
