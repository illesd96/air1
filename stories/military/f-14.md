# f-14
## Hooks
- After the 1979 Iranian Revolution, the Shah's 79 F-14s became the world's largest fleet of Tomcats outside the US Navy — Iran still flies them today, kept alive by smuggled parts and 3D-printed components.
- The F-14's AIM-54 Phoenix missile could engage targets from 100 miles away, and a single Tomcat could simultaneously track 24 targets and engage 6 — capabilities that no other fighter would match for 30 years.
- An Iranian F-14 pilot, Jalil Zandi, allegedly shot down 11 Iraqi aircraft during the Iran-Iraq war, making him history's only Tomcat ace.
- Goose's death scene in Top Gun was so realistic that the Navy actually used the film's footage in safety briefings about ejection seat hazards.
- The F-14's swing wings were controlled automatically by an onboard computer that adjusted them in flight — pilots rarely touched the wing-sweep override.
- When the Navy retired the F-14 in 2006, they destroyed most airframes with hammers to prevent Iran from buying spare parts at scrapyards — a directive personally signed by the Secretary of Defense.

## Seed paragraph

When Hollywood killed off Goose in Top Gun, the US Navy started using the scene in real safety training. But the Tomcat's strangest legacy isn't in Maverick's hands — it's in Tehran. After the Shah fell, Iran inherited the only foreign fleet of F-14s on Earth, and somehow kept them flying for four decades using smuggled parts, jury-rigged electronics, and one ace pilot who racked up eleven kills the Pentagon still won't talk about.
