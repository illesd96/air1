# f-16
## Hooks
- The F-16's revolutionary fly-by-wire system was so aggressive that early prototypes were intentionally unstable — without the computer, the jet would tear itself apart in under a second.
- The 'Viper' nickname comes from pilots thinking the aircraft's profile resembled the Colonial Viper from the original Battlestar Galactica TV show, not the snake.
- An Israeli F-16 dropped the bombs that destroyed Iraq's Osirak nuclear reactor in 1981 — the pilots flew at 100 feet to avoid radar and many didn't have enough fuel to return safely.
- The reclined seat and side-mounted stick weren't just for looks — they were specifically designed so pilots could pull 9 Gs without blacking out, redefining dogfighting forever.
- A Pakistani F-16 may have shot down a Soviet An-26 transport during the Soviet-Afghan war, an engagement Moscow never officially acknowledged.
- In 2023, a Ukrainian volunteer pilot trained on F-16s in his garage using a homemade simulator built from real cockpit parts smuggled out of decommissioned jets.

## Seed paragraph

On June 7, 1981, eight Israeli F-16s skimmed the desert at treetop height, crossed three hostile countries, and put eleven bombs through the dome of an Iraqi nuclear reactor — then flew home on fumes. The jet that pulled it off had been designed as a cheap dogfighter that engineers admitted was 'aerodynamically unstable on purpose.' This is how the world's most-exported fighter became the workhorse of forty air forces.
