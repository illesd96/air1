# p-38
## Hooks
- On April 18, 1943, sixteen P-38s flew 1,000 miles round-trip over the Pacific to ambush and kill Admiral Isoroku Yamamoto — the architect of Pearl Harbor — in retaliation for the attack.
- Charles Lindbergh, technically a civilian, flew combat missions in P-38s in the Pacific in 1944 and shot down a Japanese aircraft — teaching pilots fuel-efficiency techniques that extended their range by 30%.
- America's top WWII ace, Richard Bong, scored all 40 of his confirmed kills in P-38s before being killed in a P-80 jet test flight crash in August 1945 — the same day the war ended.
- The P-38's twin-engine design meant pilots transitioned directly from the trainer to the fighter with no twin-engine experience — early accidents were so frequent the type was nicknamed 'Pilot Killer.'
- P-38s flew the photo reconnaissance mission that confirmed Japanese carriers were heading toward Midway in June 1942 — the intelligence that won the battle.
- Antoine de Saint-Exupéry, the author of 'The Little Prince,' was a French Air Force P-38 reconnaissance pilot who disappeared over the Mediterranean in 1944 — his plane wasn't found until 2000.

## Seed paragraph

On the morning of April 18, 1943, sixteen American P-38 Lightnings took off from Guadalcanal on a 1,000-mile journey to murder a single man. Admiral Yamamoto had planned the attack on Pearl Harbor. Now American codebreakers knew his exact flight schedule. The Lightning made the impossible interception, killed Yamamoto, and gave Japan a wound it never recovered from. The same airframe carried Antoine de Saint-Exupéry on his final flight — and his plane wasn't found until 2000.
