# p-51
## Hooks
- The P-51 was designed in 117 days flat — North American Aviation built the prototype from a blank sheet of paper to a flying aircraft in under four months to win a British contract.
- Its game-changing performance came from a British engine: the Rolls-Royce Merlin, installed by mistake when an early Mustang variant proved underpowered with its original Allison V-1710.
- Tuskegee Airmen flying P-51s with distinctive red tails escorted bombers deep into Germany with a reputation for never losing a bomber under their protection — recent research shows this was nearly true but not perfect.
- Chuck Yeager scored an 'ace in a day' on October 12, 1944, shooting down five German fighters in a single mission while flying a P-51 named 'Glamorous Glennis.'
- A P-51 flown by Lt. Urban Drew shot down two Me 262 jets during their takeoff roll in October 1944 — one of the few air-to-air kills against the world's first operational jet fighter.
- Post-WWII, P-51s went to wars in Korea, the Israeli War of Independence, and the 1969 Football War between Honduras and El Salvador — the last propeller-on-propeller dogfights in history.

## Seed paragraph

It started with a British rejection letter and a 117-day deadline. North American Aviation had never designed a fighter, but in 1940 they bet the company that they could build one from scratch in four months. The plane they delivered would escort bombers to Berlin, fight in seven wars, and engage in history's last propeller dogfights over Honduras in 1969. The secret to its greatness wasn't American — it was a British engine installed almost by accident.
