# spitfire
## Hooks
- The Spitfire's elliptical wing was chosen not for beauty but because it gave the lowest induced drag — designer R.J. Mitchell died in 1937 before ever seeing his masterpiece fly in combat.
- Late-war Spitfires were so much faster than the early Battle of Britain models that the Mk. XIV could outrun a German V-1 flying bomb and tip it with a wingtip rather than risk shooting its warhead.
- Spitfires shot down 303 V-1 buzz bombs over England in 1944, with pilots developing the dangerous 'wing flip' technique to upset the missile's gyroscope.
- More Spitfires were built (20,341) than any other British aircraft, and the type was produced in 24 different variants — every one essentially a different aircraft.
- In 1947, Spitfires were still fighting — flown by both the Egyptian and Israeli air forces during the 1948 Arab-Israeli War, in some of the last propeller-on-propeller dogfights.
- A Spitfire was once buried in Burma at the end of WWII and rumored to be one of dozens hidden underground; multiple expeditions searched for the cache before concluding in 2013 that the legend was false.

## Seed paragraph

R.J. Mitchell died of cancer at 42, three years before the plane he designed would save Britain. He never saw a Spitfire fire its guns in anger. But the elliptical wing he insisted upon — over the protests of engineers who said it was too hard to build — would slice through the Luftwaffe in the summer of 1940 and later flip German cruise missiles out of the sky with a wingtip. The Spitfire was a love letter from a dying man, and Britain was the reply.
