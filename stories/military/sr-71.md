# sr-71
## Hooks
- The SR-71's fuel tanks leaked deliberately on the ground because the titanium panels were sized to expand and seal only at Mach 3 cruise temperatures of over 600°F.
- Lockheed had to secretly buy titanium ore from the Soviet Union through shell companies to build the airframe — the very nation it was designed to spy on.
- Pilot Brian Shul once outran a Libyan missile in 1986 by accelerating; he later said the Blackbird wasn't fast because it was light, but because it was 'a freight train with the soul of a sports car.'
- The SR-71 used a special fuel called JP-7 with such a high flash point that you could put out a match in it — engines were started using a chemical igniter called TEB that detonated on contact with air.
- The famous 'speed check' radio call story — where an SR-71 pilot one-upped a cocky F-18 — happened in real life and was published verbatim in Major Shul's memoir.
- When the Blackbird retired in 1998, NASA kept flying two of them as research aircraft, and it still holds the New York to London speed record set in 1974: 1 hour, 54 minutes.

## Seed paragraph

Every time an SR-71 sat on the tarmac, it dripped jet fuel onto the concrete — and this was by design. The plane was built to fly so fast that friction with the air would heat its skin past 600 degrees, and only at that temperature would the panels expand enough to seal. The CIA had a problem: to spy on the Soviet Union, they needed a plane built from Soviet titanium. So they made up a fake country and bought the ore from Moscow itself.
