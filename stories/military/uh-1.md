# uh-1
## Hooks
- The unmistakable 'whup-whup-whup' sound of the Huey's two-bladed rotor is caused by the blade tips going slightly supersonic and producing a directional shockwave you can hear miles away.
- Helicopter pilot Major Patrick Brady earned the Medal of Honor for flying his UH-1 medevac into hot landing zones five times on January 6, 1968, rescuing 51 wounded soldiers under heavy fire.
- The Huey was the first turbine-powered helicopter in US military service — the Lycoming T53 engine replaced the heavy piston engines that had limited helicopters for decades.
- During Operation Frequent Wind (the 1975 evacuation of Saigon), Hueys were pushed off carrier decks to make room for more incoming aircraft — millions of dollars of helicopters dumped into the sea.
- Bell UH-1s have flown in over 70 air forces and the type is still in production in updated forms as the UH-1Y Venom — making it one of the longest-produced helicopters in history.
- The 'Ride of the Valkyries' Huey assault scene in Apocalypse Now was filmed using actual US Army Hueys leased to the production — and one was lost during filming.

## Seed paragraph

The sound of the Huey isn't engineering noise — it's the sound of rotor tips breaking the speed of sound. For an entire generation of Americans, that whup-whup-whup is the soundtrack of Vietnam. On January 6, 1968, Major Patrick Brady flew his Huey medevac into the same hot LZ five times in one day, hauling out 51 wounded men under fire. Seven years later, dozens of Hueys were shoved off carrier decks during the fall of Saigon — millions of dollars in helicopters dumped into the South China Sea.
